## Module 1: Cough (dry vs productive)

**Summary:** The right approach depends on which type of cough you have — a dry, hacking, sleep-disrupting cough is managed differently to a "wet" cough that's actually clearing mucus.

**What actually helps:**
- **Honey (over 12 months) — Moderate evidence**, comparable to dextromethorphan in some trials for reducing frequency/severity. See Module 14 for full detail.
- **Suppressing a dry cough (dextromethorphan) — reasonable when cough is disrupting sleep**, though trial evidence for benefit is mixed/weak overall.
- **Not suppressing a productive (wet) cough** — clearing mucus is a useful function; suppressing it can cause mucus retention, particularly relevant for anyone with asthma or COPD (see Module 22).
- **Elevating the head at night** — reduces mucus pooling in the throat, commonly recommended, mechanistically sound though not heavily trial-tested on its own.

**Who should take extra care:** Anyone with asthma or COPD should be cautious with cough suppressants, since suppressing a productive cough can worsen airway obstruction. *Source: [Doctronic/GoodRx: Dextromethorphan contraindications](https://www.droracle.ai/articles/533722/what-are-the-contraindications-to-robitussin-dextromethorphan).*

**Sources:**
- [Cochrane: Honey for acute cough in children](https://www.cochranelibrary.com/cdsr/doi/10.1002/14651858.CD007094.pub5/full)
- [Dextromethorphan contraindications — respiratory conditions](https://www.droracle.ai/articles/533722/what-are-the-contraindications-to-robitussin-dextromethorphan)

## Module 2 & 16: Sore Throat, Lozenges & Throat Sprays

**Summary:** Sore throat relief is largely about local comfort measures — gargles, lozenges, sprays — layered with pain relief if needed.

**What actually helps:**
- Saltwater gargle — low-cost, low-risk comfort measure (see Module 15 for detail).
- Paracetamol/ibuprofen — genuinely reduces throat pain and any associated fever.
- Medicated lozenges/sprays — temporary local numbing, comfort-level evidence.

**Red flag to flag clearly on the site:** Sore throat with high fever *and* swollen tonsils or white patches raises the possibility of bacterial (strep) infection rather than a viral cold — this warrants a GP visit rather than home management alone, since strep needs antibiotics and can (rarely) lead to complications if untreated.

**Sources:**
- [CDC: Manage Common Cold](https://www.cdc.gov/) *(general home care guidance)*
- [Mayo Clinic: Sore throat — diagnosis & treatment](https://www.mayoclinic.org/diseases-conditions/sore-throat/diagnosis-treatment/drc-20351641)

## Module 9: Hydration

**Summary:** "Drink plenty of fluids" is genuine, physiologically sound advice — not just folk wisdom — though the specific claim that particular drinks (like hot lemon and honey) do something uniquely different from water is mostly about comfort, not a distinct medical mechanism.

**What actually helps:**
- Staying well hydrated supports thinner mucus secretions, easier clearance, and helps replace fluid lost to fever, sweating, and increased respiratory rate.
- Warm fluids specifically may offer a modest additional comfort effect on sore throat and congestion beyond hydration alone, though this is more subjective/comfort-based than strongly evidenced.

**Practical marker:** Urine colour is a simple practical check — pale yellow suggests adequate hydration; dark urine or reduced frequency suggests topping up fluids.

**Who should take extra care:** Infants and young children can dehydrate quickly with fever/vomiting/diarrhoea — reduced wet nappies, no tears when crying, or lethargy are signs to seek care (see Module 27 triage table).

## Module 10: Humidity & Steam

**Summary:** This is one of the most-repeated pieces of cold advice on the internet, and it's worth being upfront on the site that the trial evidence doesn't actually support it well — this is a case where subjective comfort and objective effect diverge.

**What the evidence actually shows:**
A series of Cochrane reviews (most recently 2017, updating a 2013 and earlier 2006 version) on inhaling heated, humidified air ("steam") for the common cold found the evidence uncertain — one analysis method showed a possible benefit while another showed none, no studies found symptoms got worse, but overall evidence quality was rated low due to bias risk and inconsistency between the six included trials (387 participants total). One included study even showed *worsened* nasal airflow after steam, while an earlier one showed improvement. *Source: [Cochrane CD001728](https://www.cochranelibrary.com/cdsr/doi/10.1002/14651858.CD001728.pub6/full).*

**Practical/safety notes:**
- If steam is used anyway (for comfort), a warm shower or bowl of hot water is safer than leaning directly over boiling water, which carries a real scald/burn risk with little proven benefit to offset it.
- **Cool-mist humidifiers at 40–60% room humidity** are a safer way to add moisture to a sick room overnight than direct steam inhalation, and don't carry the same burn risk. *Source: [Sleep and Sinus Centers: Does Steam Inhalation Work?](https://sleepandsinuscenters.com/blog/does-steam-inhalation-really-work-benefits-and-effectiveness-explored).*
- Humidifiers/vaporisers need regular cleaning — a poorly maintained humidifier can grow mould or bacteria and become its own respiratory irritant.

**Myths debunked:** "Steam kills the cold virus" — there's laboratory (in vitro) evidence the virus is heat-sensitive, but this hasn't translated into a demonstrated clinical benefit in real trials on people.

**Sources:**
- [Cochrane: Heated, humidified air for the common cold](https://www.cochranelibrary.com/cdsr/doi/10.1002/14651858.CD001728.pub6/full)
- [Sleep and Sinus Centers: steam inhalation evidence summary](https://sleepandsinuscenters.com/blog/does-steam-inhalation-really-work-benefits-and-effectiveness-explored)

## Module 11: Rest & Sleep

**Summary:** This is one of the strongest, most underrated pieces of "boring" cold advice — good sleep in the weeks *before* exposure to a virus measurably changes your odds of actually getting sick, not just how you feel once you're already ill.

**What the evidence shows:**
- In a controlled study where healthy volunteers were monitored for sleep habits over two weeks and then deliberately exposed to a cold virus under quarantine, those who averaged **less than 7 hours of sleep were nearly 3 times more likely to develop a cold** than those sleeping more; poor sleep efficiency (time actually asleep while in bed) showed an even larger effect. *Source: [Cohen et al., Archives of Internal Medicine 2009, via PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2629403/).*
- A separate study using actigraphy (objective wrist-worn sleep tracking rather than self-report) confirmed shorter sleep duration in the week before exposure predicted higher cold risk. *Source: [PubMed: Behaviorally Assessed Sleep and Susceptibility to the Common Cold](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26118561/).*
- Mechanistically, sleep deprivation reduces natural killer cell activity, suppresses interleukin-2 production, and raises inflammatory cytokines — all part of normal immune surveillance and response.

**Practical implication for the site:** this is a genuinely strong "prevention" module, not just a "feel better while sick" one — good, consistent sleep is one of the better-evidenced things a person can actually do to lower their odds of catching something in the first place.

**Sources:**
- [PMC: Sleep Habits and Susceptibility to the Common Cold (Cohen et al.)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2629403/)
- [PubMed: Behaviorally Assessed Sleep and Susceptibility to the Common Cold](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26118561/)
- [NEJM Clinician summary](https://clinician.nejm.org/sleep-common-cold-ID200901210000001)

## Module 12: Nutrition During Illness (chicken soup case study)

**Summary:** Chicken soup has more genuine scientific backing than most home remedies — not as a cure, but as a real modest symptom-reducer, with a couple of identified mechanisms.

**What the evidence shows:**
- A 2025 systematic review pooling four randomised controlled trials (342 participants total) found soup — particularly chicken-based, vegetable-enriched varieties — modestly reduced the severity and duration of upper respiratory infection symptoms including sore throat, congestion, and cough, likely via a combination of hydration, nutrition, and mild anti-inflammatory effects. The review is upfront that evidence remains preliminary and larger trials are needed. *Source: [MDPI/Nutrients 2025 systematic review](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/17/13/2247).*
- An earlier laboratory (in vitro, not human trial) study found chicken soup inhibited the movement of neutrophils (a type of white blood cell) involved in the inflammatory response — a plausible mechanism, though it doesn't on its own prove a clinical effect in real patients. *Source: [ScienceDaily summary of Rennard et al., CHEST 2000](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2000/10/001018075252.htm).*
- Separately, hot liquids (including soup) have been shown to increase nasal mucus flow more than cold liquids, which is a plausible mechanism for easing congestion regardless of the anti-inflammatory question.

**Bottom line for the site:** reasonable to present chicken soup as "weak-to-moderate evidence, several plausible mechanisms" — a step up from most traditional remedies, but still short of strong.

**Sources:**
- [Nutrients (MDPI) 2025: Soup as Medicine — systematic review](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/17/13/2247)
- [ScienceDaily: Chicken soup study, CHEST journal](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2000/10/001018075252.htm)

## Module 18: Antiviral Considerations (Tamiflu / oseltamivir)

**Summary:** Antivirals for flu are prescription-only and highly time-sensitive — the earlier they're started after symptom onset, the more benefit they provide, with a sharp drop-off in usefulness after 48 hours for most people.

**What the evidence shows:**
- Oseltamivir (Tamiflu) is FDA/TGA-indicated for treatment of uncomplicated flu when started within 2 days of symptom onset — clinical trials show meaningful reduction in illness duration and severity when started in that window. *Source: [Clinical trial protocol summary, ClinicalTrials.gov](https://cdn.clinicaltrials.gov/large-docs/83/NCT02532283/Prot_000.pdf).*
- The earlier within that window, the better: starting within the first 12 hours of fever onset has been associated with substantially greater reduction in total illness duration than starting closer to the 48-hour mark. *Source: [Clinical evidence summary, Droracle](https://www.droracle.ai/articles/682438/what-is-the-ideal-time-frame-to-start-tamiflu).*
- For high-risk, severely ill, or hospitalised patients, treatment should **not** be withheld even beyond 48 hours — mortality benefit has still been observed when started up to 96 hours after symptom onset in these groups, and clinical suspicion during flu season is considered sufficient to start treatment without waiting for lab confirmation.

**Practical implication:** if you or a family member are in a high-risk group (very young, elderly, pregnant, chronic illness) and flu-like symptoms hit hard and fast, same-day contact with a GP is worth it specifically because of this narrow effective window — this is one of the few home-management decisions where speed genuinely changes the outcome.

**Sources:**
- [Droracle: Ideal time frame to start Tamiflu](https://www.droracle.ai/articles/682438/what-is-the-ideal-time-frame-to-start-tamiflu)
- [ClinicalTrials.gov oseltamivir protocol background](https://cdn.clinicaltrials.gov/large-docs/83/NCT02532283/Prot_000.pdf)

## Module 20: Pregnancy — Medication Safety

**Summary:** Pregnancy substantially narrows the safe medication list — several very common decongestant ingredients are specifically flagged for avoidance, especially in the first trimester.

**What the evidence shows:**
- Oral decongestants — both pseudoephedrine and phenylephrine — are generally recommended to be avoided during pregnancy, particularly in the first trimester, due to reported (though inconsistent across studies) associations with specific birth defects including gastroschisis (an abdominal wall defect) and small intestinal atresia. *Source: [PMC: Use of Decongestants During Pregnancy and Risk of Birth Defects](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3816336/); [MotherToBaby fact sheet on phenylephrine](https://mothertobaby.org/fact-sheets/phenylephrine/).*
- Topical/nasal decongestant sprays (e.g. oxymetazoline) are also generally advised against, especially in the first trimester, though the evidence base is thinner than for oral forms.
- **Recommended first-line approach in pregnancy:** saline nasal rinses for congestion, at any stage, regardless of trimester; intranasal corticosteroid sprays (e.g. budesonide, which has the most safety data) can be added if saline alone isn't enough. *Source: [Droracle clinical summary](https://www.droracle.ai/articles/735811/are-oral-decongestants-such-as-pseudoephedrine-pse-and-phenylephrine).*
- Paracetamol remains the standard first-line option for pain/fever in pregnancy; NSAIDs like ibuprofen are generally avoided, especially in the third trimester — this should always be confirmed with a treating obstetrician/GP/midwife rather than assumed.

**Sources:**
- [PMC: Decongestants in pregnancy and birth defect risk](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3816336/)
- [MotherToBaby: Phenylephrine fact sheet](https://mothertobaby.org/fact-sheets/phenylephrine/)
- [Clinical management summary — pregnancy congestion](https://www.droracle.ai/articles/735811/are-oral-decongestants-such-as-pseudoephedrine-pse-and-phenylephrine)

## Module 22 & 23: Asthma/COPD and Heart Conditions — Medication Cautions

**Summary:** Two groups need extra label-reading before reaching for standard cold and flu tablets: people with reactive airway disease (asthma/COPD) and people with cardiovascular conditions.

**Asthma/COPD:**
- Cough suppressants (dextromethorphan) should be used cautiously, since suppressing a productive cough can lead to mucus retention and worsen airway obstruction in someone whose airways are already compromised. *Source: [Contraindications summary, Droracle](https://www.droracle.ai/articles/533722/what-are-the-contraindications-to-robitussin-dextromethorphan).*
- A worsening cough with increasing breathlessness should prompt assessment for an asthma flare or another cause rather than simply reaching for stronger suppressant doses.

**Heart conditions / hypertension:**
- Sympathomimetic decongestants (pseudoephedrine, phenylephrine, oxymetazoline) raise heart rate and blood pressure and warrant caution or avoidance for anyone with hypertension, arrhythmia, or other cardiovascular disease — this is standard, well-established pharmacology rather than a contested point.
- Anyone on an MAOI antidepressant (an older but still-prescribed class) must avoid sympathomimetic decongestants entirely due to risk of a dangerous hypertensive crisis, and must avoid dextromethorphan due to risk of serotonin syndrome — this combination has documented fatal case reports historically. *Source: [US Pharmacist: Interactions Between Nonprescription Products and Psychotropic Medications](https://www.uspharmacist.com/article/interactions-between-nonprescription-products-and-psychotropic-medications).*

**Sources:**
- [US Pharmacist: OTC/psychotropic drug interaction history](https://www.uspharmacist.com/article/interactions-between-nonprescription-products-and-psychotropic-medications)

## Module 24: Hygiene Practices

**Summary:** Handwashing has a real, measurable protective effect against catching a respiratory infection — genuinely one of the better-evidenced prevention behaviours — though the effect size found across studies is modest rather than dramatic, and evidence quality is described by reviewers as generally low-to-moderate.

**What the evidence shows:**
- A Cochrane review of physical interventions against respiratory virus spread (16 hand hygiene trials, over 61,000 participants pooled) found hand hygiene programs were associated with an 11% relative reduction in respiratory illness — a real but modest effect, with low-certainty evidence due to high variability between studies. *Source: [Cochrane CD006207](https://www.cochranelibrary.com/cdsr/doi/10.1002/14651858.CD006207.pub5/full).*
- An earlier, smaller systematic review found a pooled risk reduction in the range of 16–24% across included studies, again describing the underlying evidence quality as limited. *Source: [PMC: Handwashing and risk of respiratory infections](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7169664/).*

**Practical takeaway for the site:** handwashing is worth recommending confidently as a genuinely evidence-backed, low-cost, harm-free household practice — but it's fair to frame it as "meaningfully reduces risk" rather than "prevents infection," since even the best studies show a partial, not complete, effect.

**Sources:**
- [Cochrane: Physical measures to reduce respiratory virus spread](https://www.cochranelibrary.com/cdsr/doi/10.1002/14651858.CD006207.pub5/full)
- [PMC: Handwashing and risk of respiratory infections — systematic review](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7169664/)

## Module 28: Cold vs Flu vs COVID — Symptom Differentiation

**Summary:** Symptom overlap between these three is substantial — onset speed and severity are the most useful clues at home, but testing is the only way to know for certain.

| Feature | Common Cold | Influenza (Flu) | COVID-19 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Onset | Gradual (over 1–2 days) | Abrupt — can go from fine to very unwell within hours | Variable — can be gradual or sudden |
| Incubation | ~1–3 days | ~1–4 days | 2–14 days (commonly ~4–5) |
| Fever | Rare/mild | Common, often high | Sometimes |
| Body aches | Rare/mild | Common, often severe | Sometimes, usually milder than flu |
| Fatigue | Mild | Marked | Common, can persist for weeks ("long COVID") |
| Loss of taste/smell | Rare | Rare | Was a hallmark early on; less common with newer variants |
| Typical duration | 7–10 days | 3–7 days acute, fatigue may linger 2–3 weeks | Variable, days to weeks |

Because flu and COVID in particular can look nearly identical, testing remains the only reliable way to distinguish them when it matters for treatment decisions (e.g. whether antiviral treatment is appropriate) or isolation planning. *Source: [CDC: Cold Versus Flu](https://www.cdc.gov/flu/about/coldflu.html); [Mayo Clinic: COVID-19, cold, allergies and flu — differences](https://www.mayoclinic.org/diseases-conditions/coronavirus/in-depth/covid-19-cold-flu-and-allergies-differences/art-20503981).*

**Sources:**
- [CDC: Cold Versus Flu](https://www.cdc.gov/flu/about/coldflu.html)
