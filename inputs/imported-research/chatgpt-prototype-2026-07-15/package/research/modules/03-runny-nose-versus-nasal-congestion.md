# 3. Runny nose versus nasal congestion

**Section:** A. Symptoms  
**Reviewed:** 2026-07-15  

> **Educational information only.** This page does not diagnose illness or replace advice from a doctor, pharmacist, nurse or emergency service.

## Summary

A runny nose is excess discharge; congestion is swelling that narrows the nasal passages. Saline is low-risk for both, while decongestants mainly target swelling and should be used briefly and selectively.

## Evidence snapshot

Saline may modestly improve acute upper-respiratory symptoms. Short-term decongestants can improve adult congestion, but oral phenylephrine has poor efficacy evidence.

## What actually helps

### Saline spray or drops — Moderate

**Finding:** Can moisturise dry passages and loosen mucus with minimal systemic risk.

**Mechanism:** Changes mucus consistency and mechanically clears secretions.

**Practical use:** Use as often as the product directs. Drops are useful for infants before feeds or sleep.

**Cautions:** Keep applicators clean and do not share bottles.

### Larger-volume saline irrigation — Weak-or-mixed

**Finding:** May improve congestion and discharge, but acute-cold trials are small and at risk of bias.

**Mechanism:** Mechanical flushing removes mucus and irritants.

**Practical use:** Use premixed sachets or an isotonic recipe supplied by the device manufacturer; use only distilled, sterile, or boiled-and-cooled water.

**Cautions:** Incorrect water or device hygiene can cause rare severe infections.

### Oral pseudoephedrine — Moderate

**Finding:** Can provide short-term congestion relief in adults and older adolescents.

**Mechanism:** Systemic vasoconstriction reduces swollen nasal blood vessels.

**Practical use:** In Australia it is pharmacist-only or prescription-only depending on formulation; ask the pharmacist whether it is suitable.

**Cautions:** Can raise heart rate or blood pressure and worsen insomnia, anxiety, urinary retention or glaucoma.

### Oral phenylephrine — Weak-or-mixed

**Finding:** The US FDA's extensive review concluded that standard oral doses are not effective as nasal decongestants, although Australian products may still contain it.

**Mechanism:** Oral bioavailability is too low to produce reliable decongestion at marketed doses.

**Practical use:** Read the active ingredients; do not assume a 'PE' product is equivalent to pseudoephedrine.

**Cautions:** Still has sympathomimetic cautions despite doubtful efficacy.

### Topical decongestant sprays — Moderate

**Finding:** Oxymetazoline or xylometazoline can rapidly reduce congestion for a few nights.

**Mechanism:** Local vasoconstriction.

**Practical use:** Use only for the shortest label-approved period, commonly no more than 3 days unless a clinician advises otherwise.

**Cautions:** Longer use can cause rebound congestion; keep away from young children unless specifically advised.

## Practical steps

- Blow gently, one nostril at a time.
- Use saline before feeds and sleep in infants.
- Treat allergy differently from a viral cold; persistent itch/sneezing may need antihistamine or steroid nasal-spray advice.
- Sleep with the head slightly elevated if congestion is worse lying flat.

## Who should avoid it or take extra care

- One-sided persistent discharge, especially foul-smelling in a child, may indicate a foreign body.
- Severe facial pain, eye swelling, vision change or severe headache needs urgent review.
- Do not use medicated nasal sprays for longer than directed.

## Common myths

- **Myth:** Green mucus proves a bacterial sinus infection.  
  **Reality:** Mucus often changes colour as immune cells accumulate; colour alone does not identify bacterial infection.
- **Myth:** More decongestant works better.  
  **Reality:** Higher or repeated doses increase cardiovascular and nervous-system risks without guaranteeing better relief.

## Red flags

- Severe breathing difficulty
- Eye swelling, vision change or intense facial pain
- Fluid or blood after head injury
- One-sided blockage that does not improve
- Symptoms lasting over 10 days without improvement or worsening after initial recovery

## Unsettled science / evidence gaps

- The magnitude of benefit from saline irrigation in uncomplicated acute colds remains uncertain.
