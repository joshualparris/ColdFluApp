# 11. Rest, sleep and positioning

**Section:** B. Interventions  
**Reviewed:** 2026-07-15  

> **Educational information only.** This page does not diagnose illness or replace advice from a doctor, pharmacist, nurse or emergency service.

## Summary

Sleep and reduced workload support recovery and make symptoms easier to tolerate. Positioning can improve comfort, but strict bed rest or forcing sleep is unnecessary.

## Evidence snapshot

Direct cold-treatment trials are limited, but sleep is strongly linked to immune regulation and symptom tolerance. Elevation is a comfort strategy rather than a cure.

## What actually helps

### Adequate sleep — Moderate

**Finding:** Supports immune function, mood and pain tolerance during illness.

**Mechanism:** Sleep coordinates immune signalling and recovery processes.

**Practical use:** Protect a normal bedtime, reduce evening stimulation and nap briefly if needed.

**Cautions:** Sedating cold medicines can impair driving and interact with alcohol or other sedatives.

### Upper-body elevation — Moderate

**Finding:** May reduce post-nasal drip, reflux-triggered cough and the sensation of blockage while lying down.

**Mechanism:** Gravity reduces pooling and reflux.

**Practical use:** Raise the upper torso with a wedge or adjust the bed; avoid piling loose pillows around infants.

**Cautions:** Infants should sleep flat on a firm, safe sleep surface; do not incline infant sleep products.

### Side sleeping — Traditional-but-untested

**Finding:** Some adults breathe more comfortably on one side or with the more open nostril uppermost.

**Mechanism:** Nasal cycling and gravity change congestion between nostrils.

**Practical use:** Use the position that feels easiest.

**Cautions:** No special position treats the virus.

### Prolonged complete bed rest — Weak-or-mixed

**Finding:** May worsen stiffness, sleep timing and deconditioning after the acute phase.

**Mechanism:** —

**Practical use:** Get up for fluids, toileting and brief gentle movement when safe.

**Cautions:** Stop activity if dizzy, faint or breathless.

## Practical steps

- Create a simple night setup: water, tissues, thermometer and medicines log.
- Keep the room comfortably cool and dark.
- Use a shower or saline before bed rather than repeatedly waking for multiple remedies.
- Resume normal activity in stages as fever and systemic symptoms settle.

## Who should avoid it or take extra care

- Do not give sedating antihistamines to children to make them sleep.
- Persistent inability to sleep because of breathlessness, chest pain or severe cough needs assessment.

## Common myths

- **Myth:** You must stay in bed until every symptom is gone.  
  **Reality:** Rest matters, but gentle movement is reasonable once fever and severe symptoms have eased.
- **Myth:** Sleeping upright drains the infection.  
  **Reality:** Elevation may improve comfort but does not remove the virus.

## Red flags

- Breathlessness when lying flat
- Repeated waking gasping or with chest pain
- Confusion or difficulty waking
- Infant placed in an unsafe inclined sleep position

## Unsettled science / evidence gaps

- There is little direct evidence comparing specific sleep positions for ordinary viral upper-respiratory infections.
