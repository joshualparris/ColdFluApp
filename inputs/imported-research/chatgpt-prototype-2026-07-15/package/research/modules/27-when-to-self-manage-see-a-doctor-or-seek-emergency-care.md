# 27. When to self-manage, see a doctor or seek emergency care

**Section:** E. Triage  
**Reviewed:** 2026-07-15  

> **Educational information only.** This page does not diagnose illness or replace advice from a doctor, pharmacist, nurse or emergency service.

## Summary

Most uncomplicated colds improve with home care, but breathing, hydration, age, risk factors and trajectory determine when to escalate. A sudden deterioration matters more than waiting for an arbitrary day count.

## Evidence snapshot

Emergency care is needed for severe breathing difficulty, blue colour, collapse, confusion or other life-threatening signs. Same-day or early clinical review is appropriate for high-risk people, prolonged fever, dehydration, worsening symptoms or suspected influenza where antivirals may help.

## What actually helps

### Self-management — Strong

**Finding:** Appropriate when symptoms are mild, breathing and hydration are normal, and the person is gradually stable or improving.

**Mechanism:** Supports recovery while avoiding unnecessary treatment.

**Practical use:** Rest, fluids, symptom-specific measures and daily reassessment.

**Cautions:** Use a lower threshold for babies, pregnancy, frailty, immune suppression and chronic heart/lung disease.

### GP, pharmacist or telehealth review — Strong

**Finding:** Helps when diagnosis, medicine choice or risk is uncertain.

**Mechanism:** Clinical assessment identifies complications and targeted treatment.

**Practical use:** Seek review for fever lasting more than about 3 days, symptoms beyond 10 days without improvement, 'double worsening', significant pain or persistent cough.

**Cautions:** These are prompts, not absolute rules; severe symptoms need earlier care.

### Same-day urgent assessment — Strong

**Finding:** Needed for moderate breathing difficulty, dehydration, marked deterioration or high-risk influenza/COVID symptoms.

**Mechanism:** Enables examination, oxygen assessment, testing and treatment.

**Practical use:** Use GP urgent appointments, after-hours services or healthdirect 1800 022 222 in Australia.

**Cautions:** Do not drive if faint, confused or severely unwell.

### Emergency care / call 000 — Strong

**Finding:** Needed for signs of immediate threat to breathing, circulation or consciousness.

**Mechanism:** Rapid emergency assessment and support.

**Practical use:** Call 000 for severe breathing difficulty, blue/grey colour, collapse, seizure, severe chest pain, confusion or inability to wake.

**Cautions:** Do not delay for an online diagnosis.

## Practical steps

- Check breathing effort, colour, alertness, fluid intake and urine at least a few times a day.
- Record fever pattern, medicines and symptom onset.
- Note whether symptoms are steadily improving, static, or improving then worsening again.
- Use healthdirect's Symptom Checker or call 1800 022 222 when unsure in Australia.

## Who should avoid it or take extra care

- Do not use temperature alone to judge severity.
- Do not wait 10 days when breathing, hydration or alertness is abnormal.
- Do not take leftover antibiotics; most colds and influenza are viral.
- Do not dismiss chest pain, haemoptysis or neurological symptoms as 'just flu'.

## Common myths

- **Myth:** Green mucus means antibiotics are needed.  
  **Reality:** Mucus colour can change during viral illness and does not by itself prove bacterial infection.
- **Myth:** If the fever comes down with paracetamol, the illness cannot be serious.  
  **Reality:** Response to fever medicine does not rule out serious infection.

## Red flags

- Call 000: severe breathing difficulty, blue/grey lips, collapse, seizure, severe chest pain, confusion or inability to wake
- Urgent: baby under 3 months with 38°C or higher
- Urgent: signs of dehydration, repeated vomiting, very little urine or inability to drink
- Urgent: stiff neck with severe headache, non-blanching rash, coughing blood or rapidly worsening illness
- Prompt review: fever over about 3 days, symptoms over 10 days without improvement, or improvement followed by clear worsening

## Unsettled science / evidence gaps

- No single duration or temperature threshold safely replaces clinical judgement; age, baseline health and symptom pattern change risk.
