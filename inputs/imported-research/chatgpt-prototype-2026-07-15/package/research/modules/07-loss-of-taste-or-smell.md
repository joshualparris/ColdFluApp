# 7. Loss of taste or smell

**Section:** A. Symptoms  
**Reviewed:** 2026-07-15  

> **Educational information only.** This page does not diagnose illness or replace advice from a doctor, pharmacist, nurse or emergency service.

## Summary

Temporary smell reduction is common when the nose is blocked, but sudden marked loss without congestion is more suggestive of COVID-19 or another post-viral smell disorder. Taste complaints are often actually reduced smell.

## Evidence snapshot

Treating nasal obstruction may restore smell when airflow is the problem. Persistent post-viral loss needs medical review; evidence for supplements and home 'cures' is poor.

## What actually helps

### COVID-19 testing and precautions — Strong

**Finding:** Loss of smell/taste can occur with COVID-19; testing and reducing contact protects others.

**Mechanism:** Identifies a transmissible cause and guides treatment eligibility.

**Practical use:** Follow current local testing and isolation advice, especially around vulnerable people.

**Cautions:** A negative test early in illness may need repeating according to local advice.

### Saline for blocked nose — Moderate

**Finding:** May improve airflow when loss is caused by congestion.

**Mechanism:** Clears mucus and improves access of odour molecules to receptors.

**Practical use:** Use spray or safe irrigation.

**Cautions:** Safe-water rules apply.

### Smell training for persistent post-viral loss — Moderate

**Finding:** Repeated, structured exposure to several distinct odours can support recovery over months.

**Mechanism:** Encourages olfactory neural recovery and relearning.

**Practical use:** Usually involves smelling four familiar odours twice daily for at least 12 weeks; seek clinician guidance.

**Cautions:** Do not use irritating chemicals or essential oils inside the nose.

### Zinc nasal products — Strong

**Finding:** Intranasal zinc has been associated with loss of smell and should not be used.

**Mechanism:** —

**Practical use:** —

**Cautions:** Avoid.

## Practical steps

- Check whether the nose is physically blocked.
- Use smoke alarms and check food spoilage because smell loss reduces hazard detection.
- Seek review if loss persists after congestion resolves.

## Who should avoid it or take extra care

- Sudden smell loss with neurological symptoms, head injury or severe headache needs urgent assessment.
- Do not put garlic, oils or caustic substances inside the nose.

## Common myths

- **Myth:** Burnt orange or other viral social-media remedies restore smell.  
  **Reality:** There is no reliable evidence that a specific food restores olfactory nerves.
- **Myth:** Taste and smell loss always means COVID-19.  
  **Reality:** COVID-19 is one cause; congestion, other viruses, medications, injury and nasal disease can also cause it.

## Red flags

- Loss after head injury
- New weakness, facial droop, confusion or severe headache
- Persistent loss with one-sided blockage, bleeding or weight loss
- Inability to smell gas/smoke creating immediate safety risk

## Unsettled science / evidence gaps

- Optimal smell-training odours, duration and adjunct medicines are still being studied.
