# 6. Sinus pressure and headache

**Section:** A. Symptoms  
**Reviewed:** 2026-07-15  

> **Educational information only.** This page does not diagnose illness or replace advice from a doctor, pharmacist, nurse or emergency service.

## Summary

Facial pressure during a cold usually comes from inflamed nasal and sinus lining, not a bacterial infection. Pain relief, saline and short-term congestion treatment can help while monitoring for eye or neurological warning signs.

## Evidence snapshot

Analgesics have the clearest evidence for pain. Saline and short-term decongestants may help congestion; antibiotics are not routinely useful for early viral symptoms.

## What actually helps

### Paracetamol or ibuprofen — Strong

**Finding:** Reduces headache and facial pain.

**Mechanism:** Analgesic and anti-inflammatory effects.

**Practical use:** Follow label and individual safety limits.

**Cautions:** Do not combine duplicate ingredients.

### Saline spray or rinse — Moderate

**Finding:** May loosen mucus and improve drainage and comfort.

**Mechanism:** Mechanical clearance and mucosal hydration.

**Practical use:** Use safe water for irrigation and clean the device.

**Cautions:** Stop if it causes persistent ear pain or nosebleeding.

### Short-term decongestant — Moderate

**Finding:** Can reduce pressure when swelling is blocking drainage.

**Mechanism:** Reduces mucosal blood-vessel swelling.

**Practical use:** Use a topical spray briefly or discuss pseudoephedrine with a pharmacist.

**Cautions:** Cardiovascular and rebound-congestion cautions apply.

### Warm compress — Traditional-but-untested

**Finding:** May feel soothing over the cheeks or forehead but has not been shown to cure sinusitis.

**Mechanism:** May relax facial muscles and provide sensory comfort.

**Practical use:** Use comfortably warm, not hot, for 10–15 minutes.

**Cautions:** Avoid burns and do not delay care for eye symptoms.

## Practical steps

- Check whether pain worsens when bending forward and whether smell is reduced.
- Use saline first, then any prescribed nasal spray using correct technique.
- Allow 7–10 days for an ordinary viral course while watching the direction of change.

## Who should avoid it or take extra care

- Severe unilateral pain, dental symptoms or repeated episodes may need examination.
- Steroid nasal sprays help allergy/chronic inflammation more than an ordinary cold and are not an instant decongestant.

## Common myths

- **Myth:** Any sinus pressure requires antibiotics.  
  **Reality:** Most early sinus symptoms are viral and resolve without antibiotics.
- **Myth:** Coloured mucus identifies the germ.  
  **Reality:** Colour reflects inflammation and immune cells more than the specific cause.

## Red flags

- Swollen, red or painful eye; vision change
- Severe headache, neck stiffness, confusion or neurological change
- Severe facial swelling
- Symptoms lasting more than 10 days without improvement, or 'double worsening' after initial improvement

## Unsettled science / evidence gaps

- The exact point at which symptom duration and pattern best predict bacterial sinusitis varies between guidelines.
