# 17. Warm compresses

**Section:** B. Interventions  
**Reviewed:** 2026-07-15  

> **Educational information only.** This page does not diagnose illness or replace advice from a doctor, pharmacist, nurse or emergency service.

## Summary

Warm compresses are a comfort measure for facial pressure, ear discomfort or muscle aches. They have little direct evidence for altering cold or flu biology.

## Evidence snapshot

Traditional use and plausible sensory relief; no strong evidence for faster recovery.

## What actually helps

### Warm face compress — Traditional-but-untested

**Finding:** May reduce the perception of sinus pressure and relax facial muscles.

**Mechanism:** Heat and touch modulate pain sensation.

**Practical use:** Apply comfortably warm cloth for 10–15 minutes.

**Cautions:** Avoid very hot packs and prolonged pressure.

### Warm neck or chest compress — Traditional-but-untested

**Finding:** May ease muscle soreness from coughing.

**Mechanism:** Muscle relaxation and counter-irritation.

**Practical use:** Use a wrapped heat pack on intact skin.

**Cautions:** Avoid sleeping on electric heat packs.

### Warm ear compress — Traditional-but-untested

**Finding:** May soothe earache temporarily.

**Mechanism:** Sensory comfort.

**Practical use:** Hold warm cloth against the outer ear.

**Cautions:** Does not treat middle-ear infection; no drops or substances into the ear without advice.

## Practical steps

- Test temperature on your inner wrist first.
- Use brief sessions and check skin afterward.
- Combine with evidence-based pain relief if appropriate.

## Who should avoid it or take extra care

- Reduced sensation, diabetes, poor circulation and very young children increase burn risk.
- Do not delay assessment for severe ear or eye symptoms.

## Common myths

- **Myth:** Heat draws infection out.  
  **Reality:** External heat may soothe pain but does not extract viruses or bacteria.
- **Myth:** A hot compress opens the sinuses permanently.  
  **Reality:** Any improvement is temporary comfort, not a structural or antimicrobial effect.

## Red flags

- Burn or blistering
- Eye swelling or vision change
- Swelling behind the ear, ear discharge or severe worsening pain

## Unsettled science / evidence gaps

- There are few high-quality trials specifically testing warm compresses for acute respiratory infections.
