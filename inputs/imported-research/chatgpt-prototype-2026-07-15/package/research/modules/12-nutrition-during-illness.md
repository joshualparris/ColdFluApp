# 12. Nutrition during illness

**Section:** B. Interventions  
**Reviewed:** 2026-07-15  

> **Educational information only.** This page does not diagnose illness or replace advice from a doctor, pharmacist, nurse or emergency service.

## Summary

There is no special diet that cures a cold or influenza. Eat according to appetite, prioritising fluids, easy-to-tolerate foods, protein and overall nutritional adequacy.

## Evidence snapshot

Evidence for chicken soup or individual foods changing disease duration is weak. Adequate nutrition matters most when intake is poor or deficiency exists.

## What actually helps

### Normal balanced food as tolerated — Strong

**Finding:** Supports energy, protein and micronutrient needs without requiring a special protocol.

**Mechanism:** Provides substrates for immune and tissue function.

**Practical use:** Use small meals: soup, oats, toast, rice, fruit, vegetables, eggs, legumes or other tolerated protein.

**Cautions:** Adjust for allergies, swallowing problems and medical diets.

### Chicken or vegetable soup — Traditional-but-untested

**Finding:** Provides fluid, salt, energy and comfort; limited laboratory or small human studies do not prove a clinical cure.

**Mechanism:** Warmth, hydration and aroma may improve comfort.

**Practical use:** Choose a nourishing soup with vegetables and protein.

**Cautions:** Watch sodium if medically restricted.

### Protein-rich foods — Moderate

**Finding:** Helpful when illness reduces intake, particularly for older or frail people.

**Mechanism:** Supports muscle maintenance and immune proteins.

**Practical use:** Include a tolerated protein source at meals or snacks.

**Cautions:** Supplements are not automatically necessary.

### Alcohol — Strong

**Finding:** Can worsen sleep, dehydration, reflux and medicine side effects.

**Mechanism:** —

**Practical use:** Skip alcohol while significantly unwell or taking sedating/interactive medicines.

**Cautions:** Increases risk with paracetamol misuse and sedatives.

## Practical steps

- Prioritise drinking if appetite is low for a day or two.
- Use bland, soft foods for sore throat or nausea.
- Return to normal balanced meals as appetite returns.
- Do not pressure a child to eat; offer familiar foods and monitor fluids/urine.

## Who should avoid it or take extra care

- Prolonged poor intake is more concerning in infants, older adults and people with diabetes.
- Seek advice if swallowing is unsafe or vomiting prevents fluids.

## Common myths

- **Myth:** Starve a fever, feed a cold.  
  **Reality:** There is no evidence-based reason to deliberately fast either illness.
- **Myth:** Dairy must be avoided because it makes mucus.  
  **Reality:** Dairy may change mouthfeel but is not shown to increase respiratory mucus.
- **Myth:** Garlic-heavy food kills the infection.  
  **Reality:** Garlic is food, not a proven antiviral treatment for an established cold.

## Red flags

- Unable to keep fluids down
- Signs of dehydration
- Infant refusing feeds
- Unintentional weight loss or poor intake lasting several days in a vulnerable person

## Unsettled science / evidence gaps

- The clinical relevance of anti-inflammatory effects observed in laboratory studies of soup ingredients is unknown.
