# 10. Humidity, steam and showers

**Section:** B. Interventions  
**Reviewed:** 2026-07-15  

> **Educational information only.** This page does not diagnose illness or replace advice from a doctor, pharmacist, nurse or emergency service.

## Summary

Moist air can reduce dryness and feel soothing, but steam has not been shown to cure a cold or reliably shorten symptoms. Cool-mist humidifiers may help comfort when air is dry, provided they are kept clean and indoor humidity stays controlled.

## Evidence snapshot

Cochrane evidence does not establish meaningful benefit from heated humidified air. Humidifier guidance focuses more on comfort and safe maintenance than antiviral effect.

## What actually helps

### Cool-mist humidifier in dry air — Weak-or-mixed

**Finding:** May ease dry nose or throat and make congestion feel less irritating.

**Mechanism:** Adds moisture to dry air.

**Practical use:** Keep indoor relative humidity about 30–50%; empty, dry and clean the unit as directed.

**Cautions:** Dirty units can aerosolise mould or bacteria; excess humidity promotes mould and dust mites.

### Warm shower — Traditional-but-untested

**Finding:** May temporarily loosen secretions and feel relaxing.

**Mechanism:** Warm humid air and muscle relaxation.

**Practical use:** Use normal shower temperatures and a non-slip bathroom.

**Cautions:** Does not need to be extremely hot.

### Bowl-of-boiling-water steam inhalation — Strong

**Finding:** No reliable added benefit and a real scald risk, especially for children.

**Mechanism:** —

**Practical use:** Choose a warm shower or controlled humidifier instead.

**Cautions:** Burns can be severe.

### Essential oils in humidifiers — Strong

**Finding:** No good evidence that they treat viral infection, and many devices are not designed for oils.

**Mechanism:** Odour may alter perceived airflow without opening the airway.

**Practical use:** Do not add unless the manufacturer explicitly permits it.

**Cautions:** Can irritate asthma, poison children/pets or damage the device.

## Practical steps

- Measure humidity rather than guessing if using a humidifier.
- Use distilled or demineralised water when the manufacturer recommends it.
- Clean on schedule and never let stagnant water sit for days.
- If the home already has dampness or mould, do not add humidity.

## Who should avoid it or take extra care

- People with asthma or allergies should stop if symptoms worsen.
- Steam comfort should never replace asthma treatment or assessment for breathing difficulty.

## Common myths

- **Myth:** Steam kills the virus in your airway.  
  **Reality:** Temperatures that would reliably inactivate viruses would injure the airway; clinical trials have not shown a cure.
- **Myth:** More humidity is always better.  
  **Reality:** High humidity encourages mould, bacteria and dust mites and can worsen respiratory symptoms.

## Red flags

- Worsening wheeze or breathlessness around a humidifier
- Any steam burn, especially in a child
- Mould growth or persistent condensation in the room

## Unsettled science / evidence gaps

- Whether specific subgroups with very dry indoor air gain meaningful symptom relief remains uncertain.
