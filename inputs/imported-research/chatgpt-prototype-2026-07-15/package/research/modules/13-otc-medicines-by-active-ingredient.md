# 13. OTC medicines by active ingredient

**Section:** B. Interventions  
**Reviewed:** 2026-07-15  

> **Educational information only.** This page does not diagnose illness or replace advice from a doctor, pharmacist, nurse or emergency service.

## Summary

Choose one active ingredient for one target symptom whenever possible. Multi-symptom products often add ingredients you do not need and make accidental double-dosing more likely.

## Evidence snapshot

Analgesics have the clearest benefit. Pseudoephedrine and topical decongestants can help short-term congestion; oral phenylephrine efficacy is poor. Evidence for cough suppressants/expectorants is inconsistent.

## What actually helps

### Paracetamol — Strong

**Finding:** Effective for pain and fever.

**Mechanism:** Analgesic and antipyretic.

**Practical use:** Adults/12+: no more than 1 g every 4–6 hours and 4 g in 24 hours unless a lower limit is advised.

**Cautions:** Liver toxicity with overdose; check combinations.

### Ibuprofen — Strong

**Finding:** Effective for pain, fever and inflammatory discomfort.

**Mechanism:** NSAID reduces prostaglandins.

**Practical use:** Follow the pack; use the lowest effective dose for the shortest time.

**Cautions:** Caution with ulcers, kidney disease, dehydration, anticoagulants, cardiovascular disease, pregnancy and some asthma.

### Pseudoephedrine — Moderate

**Finding:** Provides short-term relief of nasal congestion for many adults.

**Mechanism:** Systemic alpha-adrenergic vasoconstriction.

**Practical use:** Australian Schedule 3/4 medicine; discuss with a pharmacist.

**Cautions:** May increase heart rate/BP, anxiety, tremor, insomnia or urinary retention.

### Oral phenylephrine — Weak-or-mixed

**Finding:** FDA evidence review found marketed oral doses ineffective for congestion.

**Mechanism:** Low systemic exposure after oral use.

**Practical use:** Check labels; 'PE' often indicates phenylephrine.

**Cautions:** May still cause sympathomimetic side effects despite little benefit.

### Oxymetazoline/xylometazoline nasal spray — Moderate

**Finding:** Rapid local congestion relief.

**Mechanism:** Local vasoconstriction.

**Practical use:** Use for only a few days according to the label.

**Cautions:** Rebound congestion with prolonged use.

### Sedating first-generation antihistamines — Weak-or-mixed

**Finding:** May slightly reduce sneezing/runny nose in adults but do not treat the virus and cause sedation/anticholinergic effects.

**Mechanism:** Blocks histamine and dries secretions.

**Practical use:** Reserve for a clear reason and ask a pharmacist if older, taking other sedatives or driving.

**Cautions:** Do not use for cough/cold/flu under 6; never under 2 for any indication.

### Non-sedating antihistamines — Weak-or-mixed

**Finding:** Useful for allergy, not generally for an uncomplicated viral cold.

**Mechanism:** Peripheral H1 blockade.

**Practical use:** Use when symptoms fit allergy: itch, repeated sneezing, watery eyes, exposure pattern.

**Cautions:** Can still cause side effects and does not shorten a cold.

### Dextromethorphan — Weak-or-mixed

**Finding:** May suppress a troublesome dry cough in some adults; results are inconsistent.

**Mechanism:** Central cough suppression.

**Practical use:** Follow label; avoid duplicate cough products.

**Cautions:** Interactions, sedation and misuse risk.

### Guaifenesin — Weak-or-mixed

**Finding:** May help expectoration in some people but evidence is limited.

**Mechanism:** Intended to hydrate bronchial secretions.

**Practical use:** Use with fluids and follow label.

**Cautions:** Do not delay review for breathlessness or blood.

### Combination cold-and-flu products — Weak-or-mixed

**Finding:** May offer small multi-symptom relief in adults/older children but increase side effects and dosing complexity.

**Mechanism:** Multiple mechanisms.

**Practical use:** Write down every active ingredient and time taken.

**Cautions:** High risk of duplicate paracetamol, sedatives or decongestants.

## Practical steps

- List the symptoms that genuinely need treatment.
- Read the active ingredients, not only the brand name.
- Avoid taking two products containing paracetamol, antihistamine or decongestant.
- Use a pharmacist for pregnancy, children, chronic illness or multiple regular medicines.

## Who should avoid it or take extra care

- Do not use adult combination products in children.
- Avoid sedating products before driving or caring for children overnight if you cannot wake reliably.
- Do not exceed label duration for nasal decongestants.

## Common myths

- **Myth:** A stronger multi-symptom product treats the infection better.  
  **Reality:** It only combines symptom medicines and may expose you to unnecessary ingredients.
- **Myth:** All pharmacy decongestants work equally.  
  **Reality:** Pseudoephedrine has better evidence than oral phenylephrine; nasal sprays work locally but require strict duration limits.

## Red flags

- Suspected overdose or duplicate paracetamol — call the Poisons Information Centre in Australia on 13 11 26
- Palpitations, severe headache, chest pain or marked agitation after a decongestant
- Allergic reaction, confusion or breathing difficulty

## Unsettled science / evidence gaps

- Evidence for many older OTC cough products is dated, heterogeneous and formulation-specific.
