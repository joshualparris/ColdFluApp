# 14. Natural and traditional remedies

**Section:** B. Interventions  
**Reviewed:** 2026-07-15  

> **Educational information only.** This page does not diagnose illness or replace advice from a doctor, pharmacist, nurse or emergency service.

## Summary

Natural does not mean proven or risk-free. Honey has the most useful symptom evidence; zinc may shorten a cold but certainty is low, while vitamin C, echinacea, elderberry, garlic and ginger have limited or inconsistent evidence.

## Evidence snapshot

Product formulation, timing and trial quality vary greatly. None of these remedies reliably cures influenza or replaces antiviral treatment for high-risk people.

## What actually helps

### Honey — Moderate

**Finding:** May ease acute cough and sleep in people over 12 months.

**Mechanism:** Soothing coating and possible antimicrobial effects.

**Practical use:** Use a small spoonful or in a warm drink.

**Cautions:** Never under 12 months; sugar and allergy considerations.

### Zinc lozenges/oral zinc — Weak-or-mixed

**Finding:** 2024 Cochrane review found possible average shortening by about two days, but evidence was low certainty and side effects were more common.

**Mechanism:** May interfere with viral replication and local inflammatory signalling.

**Practical use:** If used, start early and follow a reputable product label; avoid prolonged high-dose use.

**Cautions:** Nausea, bad taste and copper deficiency with prolonged excess; never use intranasal zinc.

### Vitamin C — Weak-or-mixed

**Finding:** Regular supplementation may slightly shorten colds in some people, but starting high doses after symptoms begin has not shown consistent benefit.

**Mechanism:** Antioxidant and immune cofactor.

**Practical use:** Meet normal dietary requirements; avoid megadoses.

**Cautions:** High doses can cause diarrhoea and kidney-stone risk in susceptible people.

### Echinacea — Weak-or-mixed

**Finding:** Results differ by species, plant part and extraction; no clear reliable preventive or treatment effect.

**Mechanism:** Proposed immune-modulating effects.

**Practical use:** Do not assume products are interchangeable.

**Cautions:** Allergy risk, especially with daisy-family allergy; interactions possible.

### Elderberry — Weak-or-mixed

**Finding:** Small studies suggest possible symptom reduction, but evidence quality and independence are insufficient.

**Mechanism:** Proposed antiviral and cytokine effects remain uncertain clinically.

**Practical use:** Do not use raw or improperly prepared berries/leaves.

**Cautions:** Raw plant parts can cause toxicity; caution with immune conditions and pregnancy.

### Garlic — Weak-or-mixed

**Finding:** Insufficient evidence to treat an established cold; small prevention studies are not conclusive.

**Mechanism:** Laboratory antimicrobial effects do not establish human treatment benefit.

**Practical use:** Use as food rather than a high-dose cure.

**Cautions:** Supplements may increase bleeding risk and interact with medicines.

### Ginger — Traditional-but-untested

**Finding:** Can soothe nausea or provide a pleasant warm drink, but evidence for shortening colds is poor.

**Mechanism:** Possible anti-inflammatory and anti-nausea effects.

**Practical use:** Use food-level amounts.

**Cautions:** Concentrated products may affect bleeding or reflux.

### Vitamin D — Weak-or-mixed

**Finding:** Correcting deficiency supports health, but taking a large dose after symptoms start is not a proven cold treatment.

**Mechanism:** Immune regulation.

**Practical use:** Use routine supplementation only according to personal needs and Australian guidance.

**Cautions:** Toxicity with excessive long-term dosing.

## Practical steps

- Ask what outcome matters: comfort, duration, prevention or severe-disease reduction.
- Prefer single-ingredient, quality-controlled products.
- Record supplements alongside medicines because interactions count.
- Stop products causing rash, wheeze, vomiting or palpitations.

## Who should avoid it or take extra care

- Pregnancy, breastfeeding, immune-suppressing treatment, anticoagulants and chronic disease warrant pharmacist/doctor review.
- Do not give herbal products to infants without clinical advice.

## Common myths

- **Myth:** If it is natural, it cannot interact with medicine.  
  **Reality:** Herbs and supplements can alter bleeding, sedation, blood pressure and drug metabolism.
- **Myth:** Megadose vitamin C cures a cold.  
  **Reality:** Therapeutic megadoses after onset have not produced consistent clinical benefit.
- **Myth:** Zinc always cuts two days off a cold.  
  **Reality:** That estimate is uncertain, trial results vary widely and side effects are common.

## Red flags

- Allergic reaction or wheeze
- Persistent vomiting or severe abdominal symptoms after a supplement
- Suspected overdose or use of raw elderberry plant material
- Using supplements instead of timely antiviral/medical care in a high-risk person

## Unsettled science / evidence gaps

- Optimal zinc formulation, elemental dose, timing and lozenge dissolution pattern remain debated.
- Elderberry evidence requires larger independent trials with standardised products.

## Sources

- [Zinc for prevention and treatment of the common cold](https://www.cochrane.org/evidence/CD014914_zinc-prevention-and-treatment-common-cold) — Cochrane (2024). Possible shorter duration, low certainty and more non-serious adverse effects.
- [Honey for acute cough in children](https://www.cochrane.org/evidence/CD007094_honey-acute-cough-children) — Cochrane (2018). Low-to-moderate certainty; possible short-term cough/sleep benefit; never under 12 months.
- [Echinacea for preventing and treating the common cold](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4068831/) — Cochrane (2014). No clear preventive benefit and inconsistent treatment results across preparations.
- [Colds, flu, and complementary health approaches](https://www.nccih.nih.gov/health/colds-flu-and-complementary-health-approaches) — US National Center for Complementary and Integrative Health (2025). Evidence for vitamin C, echinacea, garlic and similar products is conflicting, inadequate or mostly negative.
