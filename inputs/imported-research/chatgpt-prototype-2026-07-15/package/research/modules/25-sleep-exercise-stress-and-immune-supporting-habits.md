# 25. Sleep, exercise, stress and immune-supporting habits

**Section:** D. Prevention and household  
**Reviewed:** 2026-07-15  

> **Educational information only.** This page does not diagnose illness or replace advice from a doctor, pharmacist, nurse or emergency service.

## Summary

There is no instant way to 'boost' immunity, but sleep, vaccination, adequate nutrition, regular activity and avoiding smoke support normal immune function over time. During acute illness, recovery and safety take priority over fitness goals.

## Evidence snapshot

These lifestyle factors have stronger evidence for general health and infection resilience than most marketed immune supplements. Effects are gradual and do not replace vaccination, testing or treatment.

## What actually helps

### Consistent adequate sleep — Strong

**Finding:** Supports immune regulation and recovery; chronic sleep restriction is associated with greater infection susceptibility.

**Mechanism:** Sleep coordinates immune signalling and tissue recovery.

**Practical use:** Protect a regular sleep opportunity, reduce late alcohol and share overnight caregiving when possible.

**Cautions:** Acute symptoms may fragment sleep; focus on opportunity and comfort rather than perfect metrics.

### Regular moderate physical activity — Strong

**Finding:** Improves cardiometabolic health and is associated with better immune function over time.

**Mechanism:** Improves circulation, metabolic health and immune surveillance.

**Practical use:** Resume gently after fever and significant systemic symptoms have resolved.

**Cautions:** Avoid hard training with fever, chest symptoms, severe fatigue or dehydration.

### Adequate energy, protein and micronutrients — Strong

**Finding:** Prevents deficiency-related impairment of normal immune function.

**Mechanism:** Provides substrates for immune cells and recovery.

**Practical use:** Eat a varied diet; use supplements to correct a documented or likely deficiency rather than megadosing.

**Cautions:** High-dose supplements can cause toxicity and interactions.

### Stress regulation and social support — Moderate

**Finding:** Chronic stress can impair sleep, health behaviours and aspects of immune regulation.

**Mechanism:** Reduces sustained stress activation and supports adherence.

**Practical use:** Use brief breathing, daylight, practical help and compassionate expectations while sick.

**Cautions:** Not a cure and not grounds for blaming someone whose illness persists.

### Avoid smoking and vaping — Strong

**Finding:** Reduces airway irritation and long-term respiratory risk.

**Mechanism:** Protects mucociliary function and airway tissue.

**Practical use:** Keep the home and car smoke-free.

**Cautions:** Seek cessation support rather than relying on willpower alone.

## Practical steps

- Prioritise one extra sleep opportunity rather than buying an 'immune' product.
- Eat simple foods with carbohydrate, protein and fruit or vegetables as appetite allows.
- Use daylight and gentle movement once improving to re-establish rhythm.
- Return to exercise progressively after fever, chest symptoms and marked fatigue settle.

## Who should avoid it or take extra care

- Do not exercise hard to 'sweat out' a fever.
- Avoid mega-dose vitamin C, zinc or vitamin D without a clear reason.
- Avoid interpreting stress or attitude as the cause of infection or failed recovery.

## Common myths

- **Myth:** You can boost the immune system overnight.  
  **Reality:** Immune function is complex; healthy routines support it, but no food or supplement provides instant broad protection.
- **Myth:** Sweating or intense exercise clears a virus faster.  
  **Reality:** It can worsen dehydration and strain the body while systemically unwell.

## Red flags

- Chest pain, fainting, unusual breathlessness or palpitations during return to exercise
- Severe fatigue that persists or worsens well beyond the acute illness
- Signs of disordered supplement use or possible overdose

## Unsettled science / evidence gaps

- Associations between lifestyle factors and infection risk do not always prove a specific causal effect or predict benefit from changing one factor during an existing cold.
