# 19. Infants and young children

**Section:** C. Special populations  
**Reviewed:** 2026-07-15  

> **Educational information only.** This page does not diagnose illness or replace advice from a doctor, pharmacist, nurse or emergency service.

## Summary

Young children usually need supportive care rather than cough-and-cold medicines. Age-specific fever and breathing signs matter more than the exact label of the virus.

## Evidence snapshot

Fluids or feeds, saline nose drops and careful monitoring are the main home measures. Honey is unsafe under 12 months, and Australian regulators restrict sedating antihistamines for cough/cold/flu in children under 6.

## What actually helps

### Frequent breastfeeds, formula or usual fluids — Strong

**Finding:** Helps prevent dehydration when intake is reduced.

**Mechanism:** Replaces fluid losses.

**Practical use:** Offer smaller amounts more often; track wet nappies or urine.

**Cautions:** Seek review if the child cannot keep fluids down or urinates much less.

### Saline nose drops or spray — Moderate

**Finding:** Can loosen nasal mucus and make feeding or sleep more comfortable.

**Mechanism:** Moistens and mechanically clears secretions.

**Practical use:** Use a child-appropriate saline product before feeds or sleep; gently wipe or suction only if needed.

**Cautions:** Avoid forceful suction, which can irritate the nose.

### Paracetamol or ibuprofen for distress — Strong

**Finding:** Can relieve fever-related discomfort and pain; the goal is comfort, not forcing temperature to normal.

**Mechanism:** Analgesic and antipyretic effects.

**Practical use:** Dose by the child's current weight and the product label. Ibuprofen is generally for children older than 3 months unless a clinician advises otherwise.

**Cautions:** Do not alternate routinely without professional advice; avoid ibuprofen with dehydration or certain medical conditions.

### Honey for cough — Moderate

**Finding:** May reduce night cough in children older than 12 months.

**Mechanism:** Soothing and possible antioxidant/antimicrobial effects.

**Practical use:** A small spoonful before bed can be tried from 12 months of age.

**Cautions:** Never give honey to a baby under 12 months because of infant botulism risk.

### OTC cough, cold and decongestant medicines — Weak-or-mixed

**Finding:** There is little evidence of meaningful benefit in young children and a real risk of dosing errors and adverse effects.

**Mechanism:** Varies by ingredient.

**Practical use:** Do not use unless a doctor or pharmacist specifically recommends an age-appropriate product.

**Cautions:** First-generation sedating antihistamines must not be used for cough/cold/flu in children under 6 in Australia; adult products are inappropriate.

## Practical steps

- Keep the child close enough to notice work of breathing, colour, alertness and fluid intake.
- Use saline before feeds if congestion is making sucking difficult.
- Let the child rest, but use normal safe-sleep practices: on a flat, firm surface without pillows or improvised elevation for babies.
- Write down medicine name, strength, dose and time to prevent accidental repeat dosing.

## Who should avoid it or take extra care

- No honey under 12 months.
- No aspirin in children unless specifically prescribed.
- Do not use steam bowls, essential oils near the face, or adult vapour rubs in babies.
- Avoid cold baths, stripping the child or trying to 'sweat out' a fever.

## Common myths

- **Myth:** A high fever number alone tells you how sick a child is.  
  **Reality:** Age, breathing, alertness, hydration and the overall pattern are crucial; any fever in a baby under 3 months needs urgent assessment.
- **Myth:** Cough syrup helps young children sleep and recover.  
  **Reality:** Benefit is unproven for most products, while sedation and dosing harms are well documented.

## Red flags

- Baby younger than 3 months with a temperature of 38°C or higher — urgent medical assessment
- Blue, grey or very pale colour; grunting; ribs sucking in; pauses in breathing; severe wheeze or stridor
- Very sleepy, difficult to wake, floppy, confused, inconsolable or having a seizure
- Poor feeding with fewer wet nappies, no tears, dry mouth or sunken eyes
- Symptoms worsening, or not starting to improve after about 48 hours in a young child

## Unsettled science / evidence gaps

- Evidence for nasal suction devices is limited; gentle use may help selected infants, but repeated or forceful suction can worsen irritation.
