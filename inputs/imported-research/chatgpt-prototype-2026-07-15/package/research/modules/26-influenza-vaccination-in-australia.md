# 26. Influenza vaccination in Australia

**Section:** D. Prevention and household  
**Reviewed:** 2026-07-15  

> **Educational information only.** This page does not diagnose illness or replace advice from a doctor, pharmacist, nurse or emergency service.

## Summary

Annual influenza vaccination is recommended for everyone aged 6 months and older who can receive it. It cannot cause influenza and remains worthwhile even if given after the usual autumn campaign.

## Evidence snapshot

Australia updates influenza vaccines each year. The National Immunisation Program funds vaccination for specified groups at higher risk of severe influenza.

## What actually helps

### Annual influenza vaccination — Strong

**Finding:** Reduces influenza illness and, importantly, severe disease and complications.

**Mechanism:** Induces strain-specific adaptive immunity.

**Practical use:** Book each year, ideally before influenza activity rises; later vaccination can still protect for the remaining season.

**Cautions:** Protection is not immediate and varies with age, health and strain match.

### Vaccination during pregnancy — Strong

**Finding:** Protects the pregnant person and provides antibodies that help protect the infant after birth.

**Mechanism:** Maternal immune response and transplacental antibodies.

**Practical use:** Recommended in every pregnancy and can be given at any stage.

**Cautions:** Discuss timing alongside other maternal vaccines.

### NIP-funded vaccination — Strong

**Finding:** Improves access for groups at greater risk of severe influenza.

**Mechanism:** Publicly funded prevention.

**Practical use:** Current funded groups include children 6 months to under 5 years, all adults 65+, Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander people 6 months+, pregnant people and people with specified medical conditions.

**Cautions:** Eligibility and delivery arrangements can change; check the current Australian Immunisation Handbook or health.gov.au.

## Practical steps

- Ask a GP, pharmacist or immunisation provider which vaccine formulation is appropriate.
- Do not delay solely because autumn has passed; influenza can circulate later.
- Allow about two weeks for the immune response to develop.
- Children receiving influenza vaccine for the first time may need two doses depending on age and history; confirm with the provider.

## Who should avoid it or take extra care

- People with a history of severe allergic reaction to a vaccine component need specialist advice.
- Minor cold symptoms without fever usually do not prevent vaccination, but significant acute illness may justify rescheduling.
- Vaccination does not replace staying home when infectious.

## Common myths

- **Myth:** The flu vaccine gives you influenza.  
  **Reality:** Injected influenza vaccines used in Australia do not contain live influenza capable of causing flu.
- **Myth:** It is too late once winter starts.  
  **Reality:** Vaccination can still be useful while influenza is circulating, although earlier protection is preferable.

## Red flags

- Signs of anaphylaxis after vaccination — call 000
- A concerning or persistent reaction — contact the vaccination provider or seek medical advice

## Unsettled science / evidence gaps

- Vaccine effectiveness varies each season because circulating strains and population immunity change.
