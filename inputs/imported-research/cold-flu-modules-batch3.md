Covers: elderly/immunocompromised risk, psychological stress as a risk factor, exercise and immune function, sinus pressure/headache, ear pressure, and loss of taste/smell. ## Module 21: Elderly & Immunocompromised — Why Flu Hits Differently

**Summary:** For older adults and anyone immunocompromised, flu isn't just "a worse cold" — the risk is specifically secondary bacterial pneumonia, and the danger window extends for weeks after the initial infection, not just during the acute illness.

**What the evidence shows:**
- Influenza infection damages the lining of the airways and alters immune cells (alveolar macrophages) in the lungs for roughly two weeks after infection, during which the body is more vulnerable to a secondary bacterial infection — meaning **the highest risk of bacterial pneumonia is actually 6–8 days *after* the flu starts, and elevated risk can persist 3–4 weeks post-infection.** *Source: [PMC: Effects of influenza immunization on pneumonia in the elderly](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5861791/).*
- Around 90% of flu-related deaths occur in older adults, and secondary bacterial infection is implicated in the majority of these — age-related immune decline ("immunosenescence") is the underlying driver. *Source: [PMC: Diminished immune responses with aging](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6939636/).*
- Immunocompromised patients who develop severe flu-related pneumonia requiring intensive care have roughly triple the mortality risk of non-immunocompromised patients with the same severity of illness — and can shed the virus (remain contagious) for a substantially longer period, sometimes months. *Source: [PMC: Influenza pneumonia in critically-ill immunocompromised patients](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9750560/).*
- Vaccination remains protective in these groups even though it's typically less effective than in younger, healthy adults — meta-analyses show flu vaccination reduces pneumonia- and flu-related hospitalisation by roughly 25–53% in older adults, with formulations designed specifically for this age group (e.g. adjuvanted or high-dose vaccines) performing better than standard-dose versions.

**Practical implication for the site:** this is the group where "wait and see if it gets worse" carries the most real risk — a lower threshold for medical review (and, per Module 18, antiviral treatment within the 48-hour window) genuinely matters more here than for a healthy younger adult.

**Sources:**
- [PMC: Effects of influenza immunization on pneumonia in the elderly](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5861791/)
- [PMC: Diminished immune responses with aging predispose older adults to flu complications](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6939636/)
- [PMC: Clinical features and outcome of influenza pneumonia in critically-ill immunocompromised patients](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9750560/)

## Module 25a: Psychological Stress and Cold Susceptibility

**Summary:** This is one of the best-controlled bodies of research in the whole project — real experiments where healthy volunteers were deliberately exposed to a cold virus under quarantine, so it's not just correlation. The short version: chronic stress (not brief stress) measurably raises your odds of actually getting sick.

**What the evidence shows:**
- In the landmark 1991 study, 394 healthy volunteers were assessed for psychological stress, then given nasal drops containing a cold virus and monitored in quarantine. Higher stress scores were associated with a clear, graded increase in developing a cold — the more stress, the greater the risk, in a dose-response relationship. *Source: [New England Journal of Medicine, Cohen et al. 1991](https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJM199108293250903).*
- A key nuance from follow-up work: **short-term stressful events (under a month) did not increase cold risk, but stressors lasting a month or longer produced a substantial increase in risk** — this specifically implicates *chronic* rather than acute stress. *Source: [PubMed: Types of stressors that increase susceptibility to the common cold](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9619470/).*
- The proposed mechanism: cortisol (the stress hormone) normally acts as an "off switch" for inflammation once an infection is being fought off. Under chronic stress, immune cells become less responsive to cortisol's regulating signal, so inflammation runs unchecked — and since cold *symptoms* (congestion, sneezing, coughing) are largely a side effect of the body's inflammatory response rather than the virus itself, this dysregulated inflammation is what actually produces worse and more persistent symptoms. *Source: [Carnegie Mellon University: Stress on Disease](https://www.cmu.edu/homepage/health/2012/spring/stress-on-disease.shtml).*

**Practical implication for the site:** this supports framing stress management (sleep, social connection, workload) as genuine preventive health content for a cold/flu site, not just generic wellness filler — the mechanism has been experimentally demonstrated, not just observed as a correlation.

**Sources:**
- [NEJM: Psychological Stress and Susceptibility to the Common Cold (Cohen et al., 1991)](https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJM199108293250903)
- [PubMed: Types of stressors that increase susceptibility to the common cold in healthy adults](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9619470/)
- [Carnegie Mellon University: Stress on Disease (mechanism explainer)](https://www.cmu.edu/homepage/health/2012/spring/stress-on-disease.shtml)

## Module 25b: Exercise and Immune Function (the "J-curve")

**Summary:** The relationship between exercise and cold/flu risk isn't linear — moderate exercise appears protective, while very heavy or prolonged exercise (marathon/ultramarathon-level exertion) is associated with a temporary *increase* in infection risk. This is often called the "J-shaped curve."

**What the evidence shows:**
- Multiple studies of endurance athletes found a clearly elevated risk of upper respiratory infection specifically in the 1–2 week window **after** marathon or ultramarathon-distance events, compared with non-runners or less intensely trained runners. *Source: [PMC: Exercise and Respiratory Tract Viral Infections](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2803113/).*
- On the other end, moderate, regular aerobic exercise appears to modestly reduce infection risk compared to being sedentary, though a 2020 Cochrane-style meta-analysis of randomised trials (11 trials, ~500+ participants) found **no significant reduction in the number of respiratory infection episodes per year** from moderate exercise — but did find a meaningful reduction in *symptom severity* and symptom-days when people did get sick, with moderate-certainty evidence. *Source: [PubMed/Cochrane: Exercise versus no exercise for occurrence, severity, duration of ARI](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32246780/).*
- The overall evidence for moderate exercise *preventing* infection outright is honestly still rated as very low-to-low quality/certainty by reviewers — this is a case where the popular claim ("exercise boosts your immune system and stops you getting sick") outruns what the trials actually show. What's better supported is that it reduces how bad symptoms are once you do catch something.

**Practical framing for the site:** fair to present as "moderate regular exercise is linked to milder symptoms when you do get sick, though it likely doesn't prevent you catching a cold in the first place — and very intense exercise around race events can temporarily raise infection risk in the following 1–2 weeks."

**Sources:**
- [PMC: Exercise and Respiratory Tract Viral Infections](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2803113/)
- [PMC: Excessive Exercise and Immunity — the J-Shaped Curve](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7176256/)
- [PubMed: Exercise versus no exercise for occurrence, severity, and duration of ARI (2020)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32246780/)
- [Centre for Evidence-Based Medicine: Does exercise prevent or treat ARI?](https://www.cebm.net/covid-19/does-physical-exercise-prevent-or-treat-acute-respiratory-infection-ari/)

## Module 6: Sinus Pressure & Headache

**Summary:** Sinus pain during a cold is generally from swelling and mucus trapped in the sinus cavities — management overlaps heavily with the congestion modules (13, 15) rather than needing separate remedies.

**What actually helps:**
- Saline nasal rinses (Module 15) — mechanically clear the passages that connect to the sinuses.
- Paracetamol/ibuprofen — reduces pain and any associated inflammation.
- Steam/warm compress over the sinuses — comfort-level evidence only (consistent with Module 10's findings on steam generally).

**Red flag to note:** Facial pain/pressure with fever that persists beyond about 10 days, or that initially improves then worsens, raises the possibility of a secondary bacterial sinus infection rather than ongoing viral symptoms — worth a GP review rather than continued home management. *Source: [ENT Health: Smell Loss Related to Colds, Allergies, Sinus Issues](https://www.enthealth.org/be_ent_smart/smell-loss-related-to-colds-allergies-sinus-issues-and-covid-19/).*

## Module 8: Ear Pressure/Blockage

**Summary:** Ear pressure during a cold is almost always due to congestion in the Eustachian tube — the small passage connecting the middle ear to the back of the throat — becoming blocked by the same swelling and mucus affecting the nose and sinuses.

**What actually helps:**
- Treating the underlying nasal congestion (Modules 13, 15) is the primary lever, since the ear symptom is downstream of the same swelling.
- Swallowing, yawning, or gentle techniques to equalise ear pressure can provide temporary relief, particularly relevant for anyone flying with a cold.

**Red flag to note:** Ear *pain* (as opposed to pressure/fullness) with fever, especially in a child, can indicate a secondary middle ear infection (otitis media) rather than simple congestion-related pressure — this is one of the more common secondary bacterial complications of a cold in young children and warrants a GP review if pain is significant or persistent.

## Module 7: Loss of Taste/Smell

**Summary:** Temporary loss of smell (and the resulting "loss of taste," which is really a loss of flavour perception) during a cold has a clear, well-understood mechanical cause and almost always resolves on its own as congestion clears.

**What's actually happening:**
- The smell receptors (olfactory nerves) sit high up inside the nasal cavity. Swelling and mucus from a cold physically block air and scent particles from reaching them — it's an access problem, not nerve damage, in the vast majority of cold-related cases. *Source: [AFC Urgent Care: Can a Cold Cause Temporary Loss of Smell or Taste?](https://www.afcurgentcare.com/chestnuthill/blog/can-a-cold-cause-temporary-loss-of-smell-or-taste/).*
- What people call "loss of taste" during a cold is usually actually loss of *flavour* — the tongue's basic taste receptors (sweet, salty, sour, bitter, umami) still work, but the complex flavour experience of food depends heavily on smell, so food tastes "bland" once smell is blocked. True, isolated loss of the basic taste sensations without any smell loss is rare. *Source: [Cleveland Clinic: Anosmia](https://my.clevelandclinic.org/health/diseases/21859-anosmia-loss-of-sense-of-smell); [MSD Manual](https://www.msdmanuals.com/en-gb/home/ear,-nose,-and-throat-disorders/symptoms-of-nose-and-throat-disorders/loss-of-smell).*
- Resolution timeline: this typically returns as nasal congestion resolves, so it tracks the overall course of the cold rather than needing separate treatment.
- Worth noting for the site (without overstating it): loss of taste/smell was an early hallmark of COVID-19 specifically, though this has become less distinctive a symptom with more recent variants and shouldn't be treated as a reliable way to distinguish COVID from an ordinary cold on its own — testing remains the only reliable differentiator (see Module 28 in Batch 2).

**Sources:**
- [AFC Urgent Care: Can a Cold Cause Temporary Loss of Smell or Taste?](https://www.afcurgentcare.com/chestnuthill/blog/can-a-cold-cause-temporary-loss-of-smell-or-taste/)
- [Cleveland Clinic: Anosmia — causes and treatment](https://my.clevelandclinic.org/health/diseases/21859-anosmia-loss-of-sense-of-smell)
- [MSD Manual: Loss of Smell](https://www.msdmanuals.com/en-gb/home/ear,-nose,-and-throat-disorders/symptoms-of-nose-and-throat-disorders/loss-of-smell)
