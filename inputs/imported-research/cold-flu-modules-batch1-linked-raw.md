## Module 13: OTC Pharmacological Options

**Summary:** Most "cold and flu" tablets are combinations of a few active ingredients that each target one symptom. Understanding the individual ingredients — not the brand name — is what lets you match the medicine to the symptom and avoid double-dosing.

**What actually helps:**
- **Paracetamol / ibuprofen — Strong evidence** for reducing fever and pain.
- **Nasal decongestant sprays (phenylephrine, oxymetazoline, xylometazoline) — Strong evidence**, act directly at the site of congestion.
- **Oral pseudoephedrine — Moderate evidence**, genuinely effective as a decongestant, but pharmacist-only in Australia.
- **Oral phenylephrine — Weak/ineffective.** In November 2024, an FDA Nonprescription Drugs Advisory Committee unanimously concluded that current data do not support oral phenylephrine at the standard OTC dose as an effective nasal decongestant, and the FDA proposed removing it from the OTC monograph on those grounds (safety was not the concern — effectiveness was). The likely reason is poor oral bioavailability: phenylephrine undergoes heavy first-pass liver metabolism when swallowed, so very little reaches the bloodstream — which is also why the nasal spray form of the same ingredient is unaffected by this finding; it's applied directly where it needs to act. *Source: [FDA press announcement](https://www.fda.gov/news-events/press-announcements/fda-proposes-ending-use-oral-phenylephrine-otc-monograph-nasal-decongestant-active-ingredient-after); as of late 2025 this remains a proposed order in the US regulatory process — see [Spectrum News update, Nov 2025](https://spectrumnews1.com/wi/milwaukee/news/2025/11/13/the-problem-is-it-just-doesn-t-work---fda-proposes-to-end-use-of-some-otc-cold-medications-phenylephrine-cold-season-smith-pharmacy-appleton-thedacare-nasal-decongestant-).*
- **Antihistamines — Moderate for allergy-driven runny nose, weak for a viral cold.**
- **Dextromethorphan (suppressant) vs guaifenesin (expectorant) — Weak-to-mixed evidence for both**, though the choice logic (suppressant for a dry cough disrupting sleep; expectorant for a productive cough) is reasonable.

**Practical notes:**
- Nasal decongestant sprays shouldn't be used beyond 3 consecutive days — risk of rebound congestion (rhinitis medicamentosa).
- Combination products often contain paracetamol — check labels to avoid accidentally doubling up if also taking plain paracetamol.

**Who should take extra care:**
- Decongestants raise heart rate/blood pressure — caution with hypertension or heart conditions.
- Codeine-containing cough products are prescription-only in Australia (up-scheduled 2018).
- Modified-release paracetamol is pharmacist-only (Schedule 3) in Australia since 2020, due to overdose/liver injury risk.

**Sources:**
- [FDA: Proposal to end oral phenylephrine as OTC decongestant](https://www.fda.gov/news-events/press-announcements/fda-proposes-ending-use-oral-phenylephrine-otc-monograph-nasal-decongestant-active-ingredient-after)
- [AAP News: FDA proposes to end oral phenylephrine](https://publications.aap.org/aapnews/news/30657/FDA-proposes-to-end-use-of-oral-phenylephrine)
- [Journal of the American College of Clinical Pharmacy, 2025 — lessons from the phenylephrine removal effort](https://accpjournals.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/jac5.2080)
- [Pharmacy Guild of Australia: pseudoephedrine scheduling](https://www.guild.org.au/guild-branches/nsw/professional-services/health-services/pseudoephedrine-recording)
- [MJA: impact of paracetamol up-scheduling on poisonings, 2023](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC10952151/)

## Module 14: Natural / Traditional Remedies

**Summary:** The most mixed-evidence category on the site — a few remedies (honey, zinc lozenges at the right dose) have genuinely supportive trial data; others (echinacea, vitamin C, garlic) are weak or inconsistent despite being widely used.

**What actually helps, ranked:**

1. **Honey (over 12 months old) — Moderate evidence.** The Cochrane review on honey for children's acute cough (updated 2018, re-examined in a 2023 systematic review) found honey probably relieves cough more than no treatment, diphenhydramine, or placebo, though it made little difference compared with dextromethorphan. Adverse events were mild and not significantly different between groups. *Source: [Cochrane Library CD007094](https://www.cochranelibrary.com/cdsr/doi/10.1002/14651858.CD007094.pub5/full); [2023 update, European Journal of Pediatrics, via PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10570220/).*
2. **Zinc lozenges — Moderate evidence, dose-dependent.** A 2013 Cochrane analysis found a significant reduction in cold duration specifically at doses of ≥75mg/day of elemental zinc; many commercial lozenges are dosed well below this, which may explain inconsistent results across studies more than "zinc doesn't work" per se. A more recent 2024 Cochrane update was more cautious, describing overall evidence as inconclusive given wide variation in trial design. *Source: [Cochrane CD001364](https://www.cochranelibrary.com/cdsr/doi/10.1002/14651858.CD001364.pub4/abstract); [Cochrane 2024 update summary](https://www.cochrane.org/evidence/CD014914_zinc-prevention-and-treatment-common-cold).*
3. **Echinacea — Weak/inconsistent.** A 2014 Cochrane review of 24 trials (4,631 participants) found no prevention comparison reached statistical significance, and concluded the evidence for echinacea is weaker than that for vitamin C or zinc. *Source: [Cochrane CD000530](https://www.cochranelibrary.com/cdsr/doi/10.1002/14651858.CD000530.pub3/full).*
4. **Vitamin C — Weak for treatment once a cold has started; marginal for prevention** in the general population, with more established benefit specifically in people under heavy physical or cold-exposure stress (e.g. marathon runners, soldiers in Arctic training) rather than the average person at home. *Source: [NCCIH: The Common Cold and Complementary Health Approaches](https://www.nccih.nih.gov/health/providers/digest/the-common-cold-and-complementary-health-approaches-science).*
5. **Garlic, ginger — Traditional, largely untested.** Comfort value; garlic can interact with some medications.
6. **Elderberry — Weak, preliminary evidence**; commercial concentrations vary widely, making it hard to compare studies.

**Who should take extra care:**
- **Honey: never under 12 months old** — infant botulism risk.
- High-dose zinc long-term can cause copper deficiency (short-term cold use is not a concern).
- Zinc nasal sprays specifically have been linked to permanent loss of smell — stick to lozenges, not nasal zinc.

**Sources:**
- [Cochrane: Honey for acute cough in children](https://www.cochranelibrary.com/cdsr/doi/10.1002/14651858.CD007094.pub5/full)
- [European Journal of Pediatrics 2023 honey update (PMC)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10570220/)
- [Cochrane: Zinc for the common cold](https://www.cochranelibrary.com/cdsr/doi/10.1002/14651858.CD001364.pub4/abstract)
- [Cochrane 2024 zinc update](https://www.cochrane.org/evidence/CD014914_zinc-prevention-and-treatment-common-cold)
- [Cochrane: Echinacea for preventing and treating the common cold](https://www.cochranelibrary.com/cdsr/doi/10.1002/14651858.CD000530.pub3/full)
- [NCCIH: Common Cold and Complementary Health Approaches](https://www.nccih.nih.gov/health/providers/digest/the-common-cold-and-complementary-health-approaches-science)

## Module 15: Saltwater Gargles & Saline Nasal Rinses (incl. neti pots)

**Summary:** Saline gargles and rinses are genuinely useful and low-risk for symptom relief — but nasal irrigation carries one non-negotiable safety rule.

**What actually helps:**
- Saltwater gargle (¼–½ tsp salt in ~240ml warm water) for sore throat comfort — low evidence quality but essentially harmless.
- Saline nasal sprays/rinses — moderate evidence for reducing congestion by mechanically clearing mucus and irritants.

**Critical safety rule:** Nasal irrigation must only use water that is distilled, sterile, or tap water that's been boiled for at least 1 minute and cooled — never straight tap water, even where tap water is safe to drink. Municipal water treatment is designed to make water safe to *drink* (pass through the digestive system), not to be flushed directly into the sinuses, and some organisms — including the amoeba *Naegleria fowleri* — can survive standard water treatment. A documented case in 2025 involved a woman who died of a resulting brain infection after using untreated water in a neti pot. *Source: [CDC: How to Safely Rinse Sinuses](https://www.cdc.gov/naegleria/prevention/sinus-rinsing.html); [FDA Consumer Update: Is Rinsing Your Sinuses With Neti Pots Safe?](https://www.fda.gov/consumers/consumer-updates/rinsing-your-sinuses-neti-pots-safe); [NPHIC case report summary, 2025](https://www.nphic.org/news/news-highlights/2362-cdc-warns-use-safe-water-in-neti-pots-to-prevent-deadly-infections).*

**Practical specifics:**
- Boil water 1 minute (3 minutes at high altitude), cool to lukewarm.
- Store pre-boiled water in a clean sealed container, use within 24 hours.
- Clean the irrigation device after each use and air dry.

**Sources:**
- [CDC: Naegleria fowleri — sinus rinsing safety](https://www.cdc.gov/naegleria/prevention/sinus-rinsing.html)
- [FDA: Is Rinsing Your Sinuses With Neti Pots Safe?](https://www.fda.gov/consumers/consumer-updates/rinsing-your-sinuses-neti-pots-safe)
- [UCLA Health: It's crucial to use sterilized water in nasal irrigation](https://www.uclahealth.org/news/article/its-crucial-use-sterilized-water-nasal-irrigation)

## Module 4: Fever

**Summary:** Fever is a functional immune response, not itself dangerous in most cases — home treatment aims for comfort, not forcing the number to "normal." Thresholds for concern differ sharply by age.

**Age-based thresholds for seeking care:**

| Age | Seek care when |
|---|---|
| Under 3 months | Any rectal temp ≥38.0°C (100.4°F) — same-day medical review |
| 3–24 months | Fever above ~39°C lasting >24 hrs, or fever with lethargy/poor feeding/dehydration |
| Over 2 years | Fever lasting >72 hours, not responding to fever medicine, or with stiff neck/rash that doesn't fade under pressure/breathing difficulty |
| Any age | 40°C+ (104°F+), febrile seizure, confusion, or breathing difficulty — emergency care |

Only about 1 in 100 children with a fever turn out to have a serious underlying problem requiring treatment — most fevers are a normal, self-resolving viral response, and behaviour (playing, drinking, alert) matters as much as the number on the thermometer. *Source: [NCBI Bookshelf / InformedHealth.org: Fever in children — overview](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK279455/).*

**Who should take extra care:** Infants under 3 months (fever can be the only sign of serious infection at this age); anyone immunocompromised, pregnant, or with chronic heart/lung/kidney disease.

**Myths debunked:**
- "Must get the number back to 98.6°F" — not the goal; aggressively suppressing fever can blunt the immune response.
- Never give aspirin to anyone under 18 with a viral illness — Reye's syndrome risk.

**Sources:**

- [Cleveland Clinic: Kids' Fevers — When to Worry, When to Relax](https://health.clevelandclinic.org/kids-fevers-when-to-worry-when-to-relax)

## Module 19: Infants & Children Under 1 (and under 5)

**Summary:** Two rules to drill in: no honey under 12 months, and no OTC cough/cold medicine under age 5–6 without specific medical advice.

**Key rules:**
- **Honey: never under 12 months** — infant botulism risk.
- **OTC cough and cold medicines not recommended under age 5–6.** The American College of Chest Physicians has recommended since 2006 that children, especially under two, should not receive OTC cough medication, given no clear benefit and definite potential harm — a position later reflected in regulatory guidance. *Source: [American Family Physician: Honey for Acute Cough in Children](https://www.aafp.org/pubs/afp/issues/2016/0701/p20.html).*
- **Ibuprofen: not before 6 months old**; paracetamol is generally the first-line option for younger infants under medical guidance.
- **Fever in an infant under 3 months is always a same-day medical matter**, regardless of how well the baby seems otherwise.

**Sources:**
- [AAFP: Honey for Acute Cough in Children](https://www.aafp.org/pubs/afp/issues/2016/0701/p20.html)
- [CHOC: Fever Chart Guide](https://health.choc.org/guide/fevers/)
- [KidsHealth: Fever in Children](https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/fever.html)

## Module 27: Red-Flag / Triage — When to See a Doctor, Urgent Care, or ED

**Summary:** Most colds and flus are manageable at home. This module gives clear thresholds for the minority of cases needing professional care.

**See a GP (book within a day or two) if:**
- Symptoms last more than 10 days without improving
- Symptoms improve then suddenly worsen (possible secondary bacterial infection)
- Sore throat with high fever and swollen tonsils/white patches (possible strep)
- Chronic condition (asthma, COPD, heart disease, diabetes, pregnancy) with any new/worsening respiratory symptoms

**Go to urgent care/same-day GP if:**
- Fever in a child lasting >72 hours (or >24 hours under age 2)
- Fever not responding to paracetamol/ibuprofen at all
- Ear or facial pain/pressure with fever

**Go to ED / call an ambulance if:**
- Difficulty breathing, or noticeably fast/laboured breathing
- Chest pain
- Blue or grey lips, tongue, or face
- Confusion, extreme drowsiness, difficulty waking
- Stiff neck with fever, or a rash that doesn't fade when pressed with a glass
- Any fever in an infant under 3 months
- Temperature of 40°C (104°F) or higher in anyone
- Signs of dehydration not improving (very little urination, no tears, sunken eyes)
- Seizure

**Sources:**
- [CDC: Cold Versus Flu](https://www.cdc.gov/flu/about/coldflu.html)
- [Cleveland Clinic: When to Take Your Child to the Hospital for a Fever](https://health.clevelandclinic.org/kids-fevers-when-to-worry-when-to-relax)

## Module 26: Flu Vaccination (Australian context, 2026 season)

**Summary:** Annual flu vaccination is recommended for everyone 6 months and older in Australia, free under the National Immunisation Program (NIP) for specific higher-risk groups.

**Free under the NIP in 2026:**
- Children 6 months to under 5 years
- Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander people, 6 months and over
- Pregnant women (any stage)
- Adults 65+ (higher-immune-response formulation)
- People 6 months+ with specified medical risk conditions

Vaccination is recommended for everyone 6 months and older whether or not they're in a free-eligibility group. It should ideally be given from April onward each year, with peak protection in the first 3–4 months, ahead of the typical June–September flu season — though it's never too late, since flu circulates year-round. *Source: [Australian Government Department of Health: Influenza (flu) vaccine](https://www.health.gov.au/topics/immunisation/vaccines/influenza-flu-vaccine?language=en).*

**2026-specific notes:** Some states (NSW, Qld, SA, WA) are offering a needle-free nasal spray vaccine (FluMist) for children aged 2–5 (WA extends to under-12) as an alternative to injection. Several states are also running additional free programs for ages 5–64 outside the NIP-funded groups during parts of the season — these vary by state and change over time, so check state health department pages for current local offers.
