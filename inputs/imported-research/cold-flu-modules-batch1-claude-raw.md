## Module 13: OTC Pharmacological Options

**Summary:** Most "cold and flu" tablets are combinations of a few active ingredients that each target one symptom. Understanding the individual ingredients — not the brand name — is what lets you actually match the medicine to the symptom and avoid double-dosing.

**What actually helps:**
- **Paracetamol / ibuprofen — Strong evidence** for reducing fever and pain. Straightforward, well-studied.
- **Nasal decongestant sprays (e.g. phenylephrine, oxymetazoline, xylometazoline) — Strong evidence**, work fast and directly at the site.
- **Oral pseudoephedrine — Moderate evidence**, genuinely effective as a decongestant, but pharmacist-only in Australia.
- **Oral phenylephrine — Weak/ineffective.** In November 2024 an FDA advisory panel unanimously found current evidence doesn't support oral phenylephrine at the standard OTC dose as an effective decongestant; the FDA proposed removing it from the OTC monograph. Poor oral bioavailability (first-pass metabolism) is the likely reason — <cite index="9-1,5-1">the FDA has concluded oral phenylephrine is not effective, though the nasal spray form is unaffected because it acts directly at the site of congestion</cite>. As of publication this is a proposed order (not yet finalised in the US); it doesn't change anything sold in Australia today, but it's a useful thing to flag on a site: "the oral tablet form of this ingredient has been found not to work."
- **Antihistamines — Moderate for allergy-driven runny nose, weak for a viral cold.** Older sedating antihistamines can dry secretions and help sleep, but don't treat the infection.
- **Dextromethorphan (suppressant) vs guaifenesin (expectorant) — Weak-to-mixed.** Reasonable choice logic: suppressant for a dry, sleep-disrupting cough; expectorant if trying to help move productive mucus — but evidence for guaifenesin's benefit is thin.

**Practical/dosing notes:**
- Nasal decongestant sprays should not be used for more than 3 consecutive days — beyond that, rebound congestion (rhinitis medicamentosa) can occur, where the nose gets more blocked once the spray wears off.
- Combination "cold and flu" multi-symptom products often contain paracetamol. If you separately take a paracetamol tablet for a headache, you can accidentally exceed the safe daily dose. Check labels for paracetamol content every time.

**Who should avoid/take care:**
- Pseudoephedrine and other decongestants raise heart rate and blood pressure — caution for anyone with hypertension or heart conditions.
- Codeine-containing cough products are prescription-only in Australia (up-scheduled in 2018).
- Modified-release paracetamol was up-scheduled to pharmacist-only in Australia in 2020 due to overdose/liver injury risk — worth knowing when comparing to standard paracetamol.

**Myths debunked:**
- "Multi-symptom is always better value" — often means paying for ingredients you don't need, and increases interaction/overdose risk.
- "Nasal spray decongestants are all the same as tablets" — the spray form of phenylephrine works because it's applied directly to the nasal tissue; the tablet form largely doesn't.

**Sources:** FDA (fda.gov), AAP News, ACCP statement, Spectrum News (Nov 2025 update on FDA phenylephrine proposal); NPS MedicineWise (Australian codeine/paracetamol scheduling); MJA (paracetamol up-scheduling study).

## Module 14: Natural / Traditional Remedies

**Summary:** This is the most mixed-evidence category on the whole site — a few remedies (honey, zinc lozenges at the right dose) have genuinely supportive trial data; others (echinacea, vitamin C, garlic) are weak or inconsistent despite widespread popularity.

**What actually helps, ranked:**

1. **Honey (children over 1, and adults) — Moderate evidence.** A 2018 Cochrane review (updated 2023) found <cite index="14-1">honey probably relieves cough symptoms to a greater extent than no treatment, diphenhydramine, and placebo, but makes little or no difference compared with dextromethorphan</cite>. <cite index="13-1">The most recent 2023 systematic review update reached similar conclusions and found honey to be an effective, low-harm option</cite>. Simple, cheap, minimal downside for anyone over 12 months old.
2. **Zinc lozenges — Moderate evidence, dose-dependent.** The clearest finding across the research is that dose matters enormously: <cite index="21-1">there's a significant reduction in cold duration at a dose of ≥75mg/day of elemental zinc, and for people considering zinc it's best used at this dose throughout the cold</cite>. Many commercial lozenges are dosed far below this, which likely explains why some trials show no effect at all — it's a formulation/dose problem more than a "zinc doesn't work" problem. Downsides: bad taste, nausea at high doses. The most recent 2024 Cochrane update on zinc overall was more cautious, calling the evidence "inconclusive" due to wide variability in trial design — so this sits as moderate-but-contested rather than settled.
3. **Echinacea — Weak/inconsistent.** <cite index="35-1">A 2014 Cochrane review of 24 trials with 4,631 participants found no prevention comparison reached statistical significance</cite>, and <cite index="30-1">the evidence for echinacea is weaker than that for vitamin C, probiotics for prevention, or zinc for treatment</cite>. Some individual preparations may have a small effect, but it's not consistent enough to strongly recommend.
4. **Vitamin C — Weak for treatment, marginal for prevention** in the general population (more established benefit specifically in people under heavy physical/cold stress, e.g. marathon runners, soldiers in Arctic conditions — not the average person at home).
5. **Garlic, ginger — Traditional-but-largely-untested.** Comfort value only; garlic can interact with some medications (e.g. HIV protease inhibitors).
6. **Elderberry — Weak, preliminary.** Some small studies suggest symptom reduction, but evidence quality is low and commercial products vary widely in concentration.

**Who should avoid / take care:**
- **Honey: never for infants under 12 months** — risk of infant botulism.
- High-dose zinc long-term can cause copper deficiency; short-term cold use is generally fine.
- Garlic supplements: caution with blood thinners and certain antivirals.

**Myths debunked:**
- "Vitamin C megadosing cures colds" — not supported for treatment once a cold has started; modest evidence only for prevention in specific high-exertion/cold-exposure populations.
- "Natural = no risk" — zinc nasal sprays specifically were linked to permanent loss of smell (anosmia) in some users; stick to lozenges/oral forms, not nasal zinc.

**Sources:** Cochrane Library (honey CD007094, zinc CD001364/CD014914, echinacea CD000530), NCCIH, Mayo Clinic, PMC systematic reviews.

## Module 15: Saltwater Gargles & Saline Nasal Rinses (incl. neti pots)

**Summary:** Saline gargles and rinses are genuinely useful and low-risk for symptom relief — but nasal irrigation carries a specific, well-documented safety rule that's non-negotiable: never use plain tap water.

**What actually helps:**
- Saltwater gargle (¼–½ tsp salt in ~240ml warm water) — comfort/symptom relief for sore throat, minimal evidence risk, essentially harmless.
- Saline nasal sprays/rinses — Moderate-to-good evidence for reducing congestion and nasal symptom duration by mechanically clearing mucus and irritants.

**Critical safety rule (this deserves its own callout box on the site):**
<cite index="59-1">Rinsing sinuses or nasal passages must only use water that's distilled or sterile, or tap/faucet water that's been boiled and cooled</cite> — never straight tap water, even in a country with treated drinking water. This is because <cite index="57-1">while tap water is filtered and disinfected to kill most pathogens for drinking, some organisms including Naegleria fowleri can survive that process</cite>, and drinking-water safety standards assume the water passes through the digestive system, not directly into the sinuses. <cite index="55-1">A documented 2025 case involved a 71-year-old woman who died of primary amebic meningoencephalitis after using untreated water in a neti pot</cite> — rare, but fatal in the vast majority of cases when it occurs.

**Practical specifics:**
- Boil water for 1 minute (3 minutes at high altitude), then cool to lukewarm before use.
- Store any pre-boiled water in a clean, sealed container and use within 24 hours.
- Clean the irrigation device itself after each use and let it air dry.

**Who should take extra care:** Anyone using well water (no municipal treatment/monitoring) should be especially strict about only using distilled or boiled-and-cooled water.

**Myths debunked:**
- "Our tap water is safe to drink, so it's safe for a neti pot" — false, per CDC/FDA guidance above; drinking safety and nasal-irrigation safety are different standards.

**Sources:** CDC (Naegleria fowleri prevention page), FDA Consumer Updates, UCLA Health.

## Module 4: Fever

**Summary:** Fever is a functional immune response, not itself dangerous in most cases — the goal of home treatment is comfort, not forcing the number down to "normal." The thresholds for concern differ sharply by age, especially for infants.

**What actually helps:**
- Paracetamol or ibuprofen — Strong evidence for comfort and reducing fever-associated pain, at label doses.
- Light clothing, light bedding — Strong/common-sense; overdressing traps heat and can worsen things, especially in infants.
- Fluids — Strong, prevents dehydration which fever accelerates.
- Lukewarm sponging — Weak/comfort-only, and not needed if medication is working; cold water or ice baths are not recommended and can cause shivering, which raises core temperature.

**Age-based red-flag thresholds (a very good triage table for the site):**

| Age | Contact doctor / seek care |
|---|---|
| Under 3 months | Any rectal temp ≥38.0°C (100.4°F) — same-day medical review, don't wait |
| 3–24 months | Fever above ~39°C lasting >24 hrs, or any fever with lethargy/poor feeding/dehydration signs |
| Over 2 years | Fever lasting more than 72 hours, or not responding at all to fever medicine, or accompanied by stiff neck, rash that doesn't fade under pressure, difficulty breathing, repeated vomiting |
| Any age | Temp of 40°C+ (104°F+), or any fever with a febrile seizure, confusion, or breathing difficulty — emergency care |

<cite index="52-1">Only about 1 in 100 children with a fever have a serious medical problem needing treatment — most fevers are a harmless viral response and the child can be cared for at home</cite>. Behaviour matters as much as the number: a child who's playing, drinking, and alert with a moderate fever is generally lower concern than a very lethargic child with a lower reading.

**Who should take extra care:** Infants under 3 months (fever can be the only sign of a serious bacterial infection at this age — always same-day medical review); anyone immunocompromised, pregnant, or with chronic heart/lung/kidney disease should have a lower threshold for seeking care.

**Myths debunked:**
- "You must get the number back to 98.6°F" — not the goal; comfort is the goal, and aggressively suppressing fever can blunt the immune response.
- "Never give a child anything under 2 without a doctor's OK" — true for many medicines, but plain paracetamol/ibuprofen at correct paediatric dosing is standard first-line care from 3–6 months onward per most guidelines — always check the product's own age/weight dosing chart.
- Never give aspirin to anyone under 18 with a viral illness — risk of Reye's syndrome.

**Sources:** CHOC, Cleveland Clinic, Nemours KidsHealth, Mayo Clinic, NCBI InformedHealth.org.

## Module 19: Infants & Children Under 1 (and under 5)

**Summary:** The two big rules parents need drilled in: no honey under 12 months, and no OTC cough/cold medicine under age 5 (some guidelines say under 6) without specific medical advice.

**Key rules:**
- **Honey: never under 12 months** — risk of infant botulism from bacterial spores.
- **OTC cough and cold medicines: not recommended under age 5–6** — <cite index="19-1">the American College of Chest Physicians has recommended since 2006 that children, especially those under two, should not receive OTC cough medication, as there's no clear benefit and definite potential harm</cite>, a position later supported by regulatory bodies.
- **Ibuprofen: not before 6 months old.** Paracetamol is generally the first-line option for younger infants under medical guidance.
- **Fever in an infant under 3 months is always a same-day medical matter**, regardless of how well the baby otherwise seems — fever can be the only symptom of a serious underlying infection at this age.

**Sources:** AAFP, CHOC, KidsHealth, Cochrane honey review.

## Module 27: Red-Flag / Triage — When to See a Doctor, Urgent Care, or ED

**Summary:** Most colds and flus are entirely manageable at home. This module exists purely to give clear, memorable thresholds for the minority of cases that need professional care.

**See a GP (non-urgent, book within a day or two) if:**
- Symptoms last more than 10 days without improving
- Symptoms improve then suddenly worsen (possible secondary bacterial infection)
- Sore throat with high fever and swollen tonsils/white patches (possible strep)
- Ongoing chronic condition (asthma, COPD, heart disease, diabetes, pregnancy) and any new or worsening respiratory symptoms

**Go to urgent care/same-day GP if:**
- Fever in a child lasting >72 hours (or >24 hours in a child under 2)
- Fever not responding at all to paracetamol/ibuprofen
- Ear pain, facial pain/pressure with fever (possible secondary infection)

**Go to the emergency department / call an ambulance if:**
- Difficulty breathing, or breathing that's noticeably fast/laboured
- Chest pain
- Blue or grey lips, tongue, or face
- Confusion, extreme drowsiness, or difficulty waking
- Stiff neck with fever, or a rash that doesn't fade when pressed with a glass
- Any fever in an infant under 3 months
- Fever of 40°C (104°F) or higher in anyone
- Signs of dehydration that aren't improving (very little urination, no tears, sunken eyes)
- Seizure

**Sources:** CDC, Cleveland Clinic, Mayo Clinic, Nemours KidsHealth.

## Module 26: Flu Vaccination (Australian context, 2026 season)

**Summary:** Annual flu vaccination is recommended for everyone 6 months and older in Australia, and free under the National Immunisation Program (NIP) for specific higher-risk groups.

**Who gets it free under the NIP in 2026:**
- Children aged 6 months to under 5 years
- Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander people aged 6 months and over
- Pregnant women (any stage of pregnancy)
- Adults 65 and over (given a higher-immune-response formulation specifically designed for this age group)
- People aged 6 months+ with specified medical risk conditions

<cite index="65-1">Vaccination is recommended for all people aged 6 months and over, whether or not they're in a free-eligibility group</cite>. <cite index="65-1">The vaccine should ideally be given from April onward each year, with peak protection in the first 3–4 months, ahead of the typical June–September flu season</cite> — but <cite index="65-1">it's never too late to get vaccinated, since flu can circulate year-round</cite>.

**2026-specific notes:** Some states (NSW, Qld, SA, WA) are also offering a **needle-free nasal spray vaccine (FluMist)** for children aged 2–5 (WA extends this to under-12) as an alternative to the injection. Several states are running additional free programs for ages 5–64 outside the NIP-funded groups during parts of the season — worth checking state health department pages for current local offers, since these vary and change.

**Sources:** Australian Government Department of Health, NSW Health, SA Health, Health.vic.gov.au, TGA (2026 seasonal influenza vaccines page).
