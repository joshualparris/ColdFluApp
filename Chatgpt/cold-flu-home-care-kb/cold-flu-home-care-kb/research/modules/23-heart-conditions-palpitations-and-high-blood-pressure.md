# 23. Heart conditions, palpitations and high blood pressure

**Section:** C. Special populations  
**Reviewed:** 2026-07-15  

> **Educational information only.** This page does not diagnose illness or replace advice from a doctor, pharmacist, nurse or emergency service.

## Summary

Several cold medicines can increase heart rate or blood pressure, disturb sleep or worsen fluid retention. Saline, rest and carefully selected single-ingredient medicines are usually safer starting points.

## Evidence snapshot

Pseudoephedrine and similar sympathomimetics can cause cardiovascular stimulation. NSAIDs can also be problematic in hypertension, kidney disease or heart failure, so pharmacist or clinician review is worthwhile.

## What actually helps

### Saline spray or rinse — Moderate

**Finding:** Can reduce congestion without systemic stimulant effects.

**Mechanism:** Mechanical clearance and moisture.

**Practical use:** Use commercial saline or sterile/distilled/boiled-and-cooled water for irrigation.

**Cautions:** Use gentle pressure; ask for advice after sinus surgery or with severe ear problems.

### Paracetamol for pain or fever — Strong

**Finding:** Provides symptom relief without the vasoconstrictor effect of decongestants.

**Mechanism:** Analgesic and antipyretic.

**Practical use:** Follow the label and check combinations for duplicate paracetamol.

**Cautions:** Liver toxicity with overdose; may not suit everyone.

### Pseudoephedrine and oral sympathomimetics — Moderate

**Finding:** Can improve congestion but may raise heart rate or blood pressure and trigger palpitations, tremor or insomnia.

**Mechanism:** Systemic vasoconstriction.

**Practical use:** Discuss with a pharmacist before use; avoid when contraindicated.

**Cautions:** Particular caution with uncontrolled hypertension, arrhythmia, coronary disease, hyperthyroidism and interacting medicines.

### Medicated nasal decongestant sprays — Moderate

**Finding:** Work locally and can relieve congestion rapidly, but systemic absorption and rebound congestion remain possible.

**Mechanism:** Local vasoconstriction.

**Practical use:** Use only for the short label duration.

**Cautions:** Ask before use with significant cardiovascular disease; stop if palpitations or severe headache occur.

### Ibuprofen and other NSAIDs — Strong

**Finding:** Relieve pain and fever but can raise blood pressure, affect kidney function or worsen fluid retention in susceptible people.

**Mechanism:** Cyclo-oxygenase inhibition.

**Practical use:** Use only when suitable and for the shortest time.

**Cautions:** Extra caution with heart failure, kidney disease, anticoagulants, ulcers or dehydration.

## Practical steps

- Choose a single active ingredient for one symptom.
- Check labels for pseudoephedrine, phenylephrine, ibuprofen and hidden caffeine.
- Keep taking prescribed heart medicines unless the treating clinician advises otherwise.
- Ask a pharmacist to screen for interactions, including MAO inhibitors and other stimulants.

## Who should avoid it or take extra care

- Avoid oral phenylephrine as an assumed safer/effective substitute; evidence for oral efficacy is poor and sympathomimetic effects remain possible.
- Avoid combining decongestants or taking them near bedtime.
- Avoid NSAIDs during dehydration or a heart-failure flare unless advised.

## Common myths

- **Myth:** A nasal medicine cannot affect the heart.  
  **Reality:** Some medicated sprays can be absorbed and may still cause cardiovascular effects.
- **Myth:** Phenylephrine is a reliable non-prescription substitute for pseudoephedrine.  
  **Reality:** The FDA's evidence review found oral phenylephrine ineffective at marketed doses.

## Red flags

- Chest pain, fainting, sustained rapid or irregular heartbeat, severe headache or neurological symptoms
- New or worsening breathlessness, ankle swelling or sudden weight gain in heart failure
- Blood pressure markedly above the person's urgent-action threshold
- Symptoms after taking a decongestant that do not settle promptly

## Unsettled science / evidence gaps

- Cardiovascular risk varies with dose, formulation and the person's condition; product labels cannot replace an individual medication review.
