# 24. Household hygiene and reducing spread

**Section:** D. Prevention and household  
**Reviewed:** 2026-07-15  

> **Educational information only.** This page does not diagnose illness or replace advice from a doctor, pharmacist, nurse or emergency service.

## Summary

Respiratory viruses mainly spread through shared air and close contact, with hands and contaminated surfaces also contributing. Layer a few realistic measures rather than trying to sterilise the whole house.

## Evidence snapshot

Staying home while acutely unwell, improving ventilation, covering coughs, handwashing and masking during close indoor contact are practical evidence-based measures.

## What actually helps

### Stay home and reduce close contact while acutely unwell — Strong

**Finding:** Reduces opportunities to transmit infection.

**Mechanism:** Reduces exposure dose and contact events.

**Practical use:** Avoid work, school and vulnerable visitors while feverish or clearly infectious; follow current public-health advice.

**Cautions:** Caregivers may still need close contact; add ventilation, hand hygiene and a well-fitting mask.

### Ventilation and outdoor air — Strong

**Finding:** Dilutes airborne virus particles indoors.

**Mechanism:** Air exchange lowers particle concentration.

**Practical use:** Open suitable windows/doors, use exhaust fans and prefer outdoor or larger rooms when practical.

**Cautions:** Balance with weather, smoke, security and child safety.

### Well-fitting masks in close indoor contact — Strong

**Finding:** Reduce emission and inhalation of respiratory particles.

**Mechanism:** Filtration and source control.

**Practical use:** Use the best-fitting mask the person can tolerate, especially around vulnerable household members.

**Cautions:** Replace wet or soiled masks; children require age-appropriate supervision.

### Handwashing — Strong

**Finding:** Reduces transfer from hands to eyes, nose, mouth and shared objects.

**Mechanism:** Soap lifts and disrupts microbes; rinsing removes them.

**Practical use:** Wash with soap and water for about 20 seconds, especially after nose-blowing and before food preparation.

**Cautions:** Alcohol hand rub is useful when hands are not visibly dirty.

### High-touch surface cleaning — Moderate

**Finding:** Can reduce indirect transmission, especially in shared bathrooms and kitchens.

**Mechanism:** Removes or inactivates virus on surfaces.

**Practical use:** Clean visibly dirty and frequently touched surfaces with ordinary detergent; disinfect when indicated.

**Cautions:** Do not mix cleaning chemicals; ventilation matters more than obsessive whole-house disinfection.

## Practical steps

- Give the sick person their own towel, drink bottle and toothbrush.
- Place tissues and a bin near the resting area; wash hands after handling them.
- Ventilate the main shared room and bathroom.
- Avoid sharing food utensils before they are washed.
- Clean phones, remote controls, taps, handles and bathroom surfaces routinely.

## Who should avoid it or take extra care

- Do not spray disinfectant into the air or onto skin.
- Do not mix bleach with ammonia, acids or other cleaners.
- Avoid rigid isolation that is unsafe or emotionally harmful for a young child; use layered risk reduction instead.

## Common myths

- **Myth:** Every surface must be disinfected constantly.  
  **Reality:** Shared air and close contact are major routes; targeted cleaning plus ventilation and hand hygiene is more proportionate.
- **Myth:** Being cold causes a cold.  
  **Reality:** Viruses cause colds; seasonal indoor crowding and environmental factors can affect transmission, but temperature alone is not the infection.

## Red flags

- A vulnerable household member develops symptoms — seek early medical advice
- The caregiver becomes too unwell to provide safe care
- Use of cleaning products causes breathing difficulty, eye injury or poisoning — move to fresh air and seek urgent advice

## Unsettled science / evidence gaps

- The relative contribution of aerosols, droplets and surfaces varies by virus, setting and behaviour; layered controls remain more reliable than a single measure.
