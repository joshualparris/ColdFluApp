# 18. Influenza antivirals

**Section:** B. Interventions  
**Reviewed:** 2026-07-15  

> **Educational information only.** This page does not diagnose illness or replace advice from a doctor, pharmacist, nurse or emergency service.

## Summary

Prescription antivirals such as oseltamivir are relevant to influenza, not ordinary colds. Benefit is greatest when treatment starts early, especially for people at high risk, severely unwell or hospitalised.

## Evidence snapshot

Antivirals can modestly shorten uncomplicated influenza and may reduce complications in higher-risk or severe disease. They work best within 48 hours, but later treatment may still be appropriate for severe/high-risk cases.

## What actually helps

### Early clinical contact for high-risk influenza — Strong

**Finding:** Allows timely testing and antiviral prescribing.

**Mechanism:** Earlier antiviral exposure limits viral replication.

**Practical use:** Contact a GP, urgent care service or healthdirect promptly when flu symptoms begin in a high-risk person.

**Cautions:** Do not wait for severe symptoms if high risk.

### Oseltamivir (Tamiflu) — Strong

**Finding:** Prescription neuraminidase inhibitor used for treatment or prevention in selected cases.

**Mechanism:** Blocks release of new influenza virus particles.

**Practical use:** Dose and duration depend on age, weight, kidney function and indication; use exactly as prescribed.

**Cautions:** Nausea/vomiting are common; rare neuropsychiatric events have been reported.

### Antivirals after 48 hours — Moderate

**Finding:** May still help people who are hospitalised, severely ill or at high risk.

**Mechanism:** Ongoing viral replication may remain clinically important.

**Practical use:** Clinical decision rather than automatic exclusion.

**Cautions:** Do not self-source or share prescriptions.

## Practical steps

- Know whether the person is in a high-risk group before winter illness occurs.
- Call early if symptoms are abrupt with fever, aches and marked fatigue.
- Continue supportive care; antivirals do not replace hydration, monitoring or emergency assessment.

## Who should avoid it or take extra care

- Antivirals do not treat rhinovirus and are not antibiotics.
- Pregnant people are a high-risk group and should seek prompt advice for suspected influenza.

## Common myths

- **Myth:** Tamiflu cures any cold or flu-like illness.  
  **Reality:** It targets influenza viruses only.
- **Myth:** There is no point after 48 hours.  
  **Reality:** Early is best, but severe, hospitalised or high-risk patients may still benefit later.

## Red flags

- Breathing difficulty, chest pain, confusion, blue lips or collapse
- High-risk person deteriorating rapidly
- Dehydration or inability to take medicines/fluids
- Symptoms improve then return with fever or breathlessness

## Unsettled science / evidence gaps

- The absolute benefit varies by influenza strain, baseline risk, timing and outcome measured.
