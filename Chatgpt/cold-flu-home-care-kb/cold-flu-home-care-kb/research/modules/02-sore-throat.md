# 2. Sore throat

**Section:** A. Symptoms  
**Reviewed:** 2026-07-15  

> **Educational information only.** This page does not diagnose illness or replace advice from a doctor, pharmacist, nurse or emergency service.

## Summary

Most sore throats accompanying a cold are viral and settle within several days. Pain control, fluids and local soothing are more useful than antibiotics unless testing or examination identifies a bacterial cause.

## Evidence snapshot

Paracetamol or ibuprofen can reduce pain. Gargles, lozenges and warm drinks are mainly comfort measures with modest or uncertain trial evidence.

## What actually helps

### Paracetamol — Strong

**Finding:** Reliably relieves sore-throat pain and fever when used correctly.

**Mechanism:** Reduces central pain and fever signalling.

**Practical use:** Adults and people aged 12+ should not exceed 1 g every 4–6 hours or 4 g total in 24 hours; lower limits may apply with liver disease, low body weight, heavy alcohol use or frailty.

**Cautions:** Check every cold-and-flu product for hidden paracetamol.

### Ibuprofen — Strong

**Finding:** Can reduce throat pain, fever and inflammation.

**Mechanism:** Reduces prostaglandin-mediated pain and inflammation.

**Practical use:** Follow the pack; take with food if it upsets the stomach.

**Cautions:** Avoid or seek advice with kidney disease, ulcers, anticoagulants, some asthma, dehydration, pregnancy or cardiovascular disease.

### Saltwater gargle — Weak-or-mixed

**Finding:** Often feels soothing, but high-quality evidence for changing illness duration is limited.

**Mechanism:** May temporarily reduce mucosal swelling and loosen secretions.

**Practical use:** Dissolve about 1/2 teaspoon of salt in a glass of warm water, gargle and spit out. Do not swallow.

**Cautions:** Not suitable for young children who cannot gargle safely.

### Medicated lozenges or sprays — Moderate

**Finding:** Local anaesthetic, anti-inflammatory or antiseptic products may provide short-term pain relief.

**Mechanism:** Temporarily numbs or reduces local irritation.

**Practical use:** Use exactly as labelled and choose age-appropriate products.

**Cautions:** Can mask worsening symptoms; some ingredients cause allergy or numbness that affects swallowing.

### Warm or cool fluids — Moderate

**Finding:** Temperature preference varies; both can reduce discomfort and support hydration.

**Mechanism:** Moistens the throat and reduces irritation.

**Practical use:** Choose what feels best rather than forcing hot drinks.

**Cautions:** Avoid scalding drinks.

## Practical steps

- Drink regularly even if appetite is low.
- Use simple pain relief before meals or sleep if needed.
- Avoid smoke, alcohol and foods that sharply irritate the throat.
- Consider testing/medical review when strep, glandular fever, COVID-19 or another cause is plausible.

## Who should avoid it or take extra care

- Do not start leftover antibiotics.
- Difficulty swallowing saliva, drooling, a muffled voice or one-sided swelling needs urgent assessment.
- Persistent white patches may have causes other than strep, including thrush.

## Common myths

- **Myth:** Any white spots mean antibiotics are needed.  
  **Reality:** Viral tonsillitis, glandular fever and thrush can also cause white patches. Diagnosis requires the whole clinical picture and sometimes testing.
- **Myth:** Hotter drinks kill the virus.  
  **Reality:** Safe warm drinks may soothe; temperatures capable of killing a virus would burn tissue.

## Red flags

- Breathing difficulty, drooling or inability to swallow fluids
- Severe one-sided throat pain, neck swelling or muffled 'hot potato' voice
- Stiff neck, non-blanching rash, confusion or severe dehydration
- Symptoms that persist, rapidly worsen or recur

## Unsettled science / evidence gaps

- The clinical importance of antiseptic throat sprays is uncertain; symptom relief does not establish faster recovery.
