# 21. Older adults and immunocompromised people

**Section:** C. Special populations  
**Reviewed:** 2026-07-15  

> **Educational information only.** This page does not diagnose illness or replace advice from a doctor, pharmacist, nurse or emergency service.

## Summary

People who are older or immunocompromised have a lower margin for dehydration, pneumonia and medication side effects. Seek advice earlier rather than relying on symptom severity alone.

## Evidence snapshot

Early testing and prescription antiviral assessment can matter for influenza or COVID-19. Medication review is essential because sedatives, anticholinergics, NSAIDs and decongestants can cause disproportionate harm.

## What actually helps

### Early clinical contact for flu-like illness — Strong

**Finding:** High-risk people may benefit from early testing, monitoring and antiviral treatment.

**Mechanism:** Earlier treatment can limit viral replication and complications.

**Practical use:** Contact a GP or urgent-care service promptly, particularly within the first 48 hours of influenza symptoms.

**Cautions:** Do not wait for severe fever; older adults may have muted fever responses.

### Hydration and intake monitoring — Strong

**Finding:** Prevents or identifies dehydration, which can worsen confusion, kidney function and falls.

**Mechanism:** Maintains circulation and renal function.

**Practical use:** Offer regular fluids if not fluid-restricted; track intake, urine and alertness.

**Cautions:** People with heart or kidney failure need an individual fluid plan.

### Medication reconciliation — Strong

**Finding:** Reduces duplicate ingredients, interactions and adverse effects.

**Mechanism:** Avoids additive pharmacological effects.

**Practical use:** Use one written list and ask a pharmacist to review cold-and-flu choices.

**Cautions:** Extra caution with anticoagulants, sedatives, blood-pressure medicines, kidney disease and glaucoma.

### Vaccination and household prevention — Strong

**Finding:** Influenza vaccination and practical infection-control measures reduce risk of severe disease and transmission.

**Mechanism:** Primes immune responses and reduces exposure.

**Practical use:** Annual influenza vaccination and current recommended COVID vaccination; improve ventilation and avoid close contact when ill.

**Cautions:** Immune response may be weaker but vaccination still provides important protection.

## Practical steps

- Record baseline cognition, mobility, temperature, breathing and fluid intake so changes are noticeable.
- Arrange someone to check in if the person lives alone.
- Keep a pulse oximeter only if a clinician has explained how to interpret it; symptoms still matter.
- Have an up-to-date medicine and allergy list ready for telehealth or urgent care.

## Who should avoid it or take extra care

- Avoid sedating antihistamines unless specifically advised; they can cause confusion, urinary retention and falls.
- Avoid NSAIDs without advice in kidney disease, dehydration, ulcers, anticoagulant use or heart failure.
- Avoid decongestants with uncontrolled hypertension, arrhythmia or urinary retention.
- Do not assume absence of fever means absence of serious infection.

## Common myths

- **Myth:** It is safe to watch and wait until an older person develops a high fever.  
  **Reality:** Serious infection may present with weakness, confusion, falls or reduced intake without a dramatic fever.
- **Myth:** OTC means low-risk.  
  **Reality:** Common cold medicines can interact with regular medicines and worsen cognition, blood pressure, kidneys or bladder function.

## Red flags

- New confusion, collapse, marked weakness or inability to manage usual activities
- Breathlessness, chest pain, blue lips, oxygen level below the person's clinician-set threshold or rapid deterioration
- Very low intake, little urine, dizziness or inability to keep medicines down
- Fever or respiratory symptoms after transplant, chemotherapy, high-dose steroids or another major immune-suppressing treatment

## Unsettled science / evidence gaps

- Exact thresholds for antiviral treatment depend on the cause, immune condition, local circulation and current Australian guidance; individual assessment is required.
