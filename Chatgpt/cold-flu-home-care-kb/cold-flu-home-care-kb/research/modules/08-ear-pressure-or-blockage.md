# 8. Ear pressure or blockage

**Section:** A. Symptoms  
**Reviewed:** 2026-07-15  

> **Educational information only.** This page does not diagnose illness or replace advice from a doctor, pharmacist, nurse or emergency service.

## Summary

Colds can swell the Eustachian tube and trap pressure or fluid behind the eardrum. Most cases resolve as nasal inflammation settles; pain, discharge or significant hearing loss may indicate an ear infection or another problem.

## Evidence snapshot

Time, swallowing and nasal symptom control are usually sufficient. Routine decongestants and antihistamines have not shown reliable benefit for middle-ear fluid in children.

## What actually helps

### Swallowing, yawning and chewing — Moderate

**Finding:** Can gently open the Eustachian tube and equalise pressure.

**Mechanism:** Activates muscles that open the tube.

**Practical use:** Sip water, chew sugar-free gum if age-appropriate, or yawn.

**Cautions:** Do not force pressure if painful.

### Gentle autoinflation — Weak-or-mixed

**Finding:** May help some older children and adults who can perform it safely.

**Mechanism:** Raises nasopharyngeal pressure to open the tube.

**Practical use:** Pinch the nose and blow very gently against closed nostrils, or use a purpose-built balloon device.

**Cautions:** Stop if painful; do not use with severe congestion, recent ear surgery or uncertainty.

### Pain relief — Strong

**Finding:** Paracetamol or ibuprofen can relieve earache while the cause settles.

**Mechanism:** Analgesic effect.

**Practical use:** Follow label and safety limits.

**Cautions:** Ear pain in young infants warrants assessment.

### Decongestants or antihistamines — Weak-or-mixed

**Finding:** May help nasal symptoms in selected adults but do not reliably clear middle-ear fluid, especially in children.

**Mechanism:** —

**Practical use:** Do not give children cold medicines for this purpose.

**Cautions:** Usual cardiovascular, sedation and rebound cautions.

## Practical steps

- Avoid inserting cotton buds or drops unless the eardrum and diagnosis are known.
- Keep the child upright for comfort and monitor hearing and balance.
- Reassess if pain or fever is increasing rather than settling.

## Who should avoid it or take extra care

- Ear discharge can mean a perforated eardrum or outer-ear infection.
- Sudden hearing loss is urgent even without pain.

## Common myths

- **Myth:** The ear needs to be cleaned out with a cotton bud.  
  **Reality:** Cold-related pressure is behind the eardrum; inserting objects can injure the canal and push wax deeper.
- **Myth:** Antibiotics are always needed for ear pain.  
  **Reality:** Many middle-ear infections and pressure problems resolve without antibiotics; assessment depends on age, severity and findings.

## Red flags

- Sudden or severe hearing loss
- Swelling/redness behind the ear or ear pushed outward
- Severe dizziness, facial weakness or neurological symptoms
- Discharge with significant pain or fever
- Young infant with fever or marked distress

## Unsettled science / evidence gaps

- Evidence for home autoinflation during acute viral illness is limited and technique-dependent.
