# 15. Saltwater gargles and saline nasal care

**Section:** B. Interventions  
**Reviewed:** 2026-07-15  

> **Educational information only.** This page does not diagnose illness or replace advice from a doctor, pharmacist, nurse or emergency service.

## Summary

Saline is inexpensive and low-risk when prepared and used correctly. It can soothe a throat, moisturise the nose and mechanically clear mucus, but it is not a guaranteed antiviral treatment.

## Evidence snapshot

Nasal saline has possible symptom benefit with low-certainty evidence. Gargling is widely recommended for comfort but lacks strong evidence for shortening illness.

## What actually helps

### Saltwater gargle — Weak-or-mixed

**Finding:** May temporarily soothe sore throat and loosen mucus.

**Mechanism:** Osmotic and mechanical soothing effects.

**Practical use:** Mix about 1/2 teaspoon salt in a glass of warm water, gargle and spit.

**Cautions:** Not for children unable to gargle safely.

### Saline nasal spray — Moderate

**Finding:** Low-risk option for dryness, runny nose and congestion.

**Mechanism:** Moistens mucosa and loosens secretions.

**Practical use:** Use according to the bottle; clean the nozzle.

**Cautions:** Do not share.

### Saline irrigation — Weak-or-mixed

**Finding:** May improve acute nasal symptoms in some adults and children.

**Mechanism:** Flushes mucus and irritants.

**Practical use:** Use a manufactured sachet/device where possible; only distilled, sterile, or previously boiled and cooled water.

**Cautions:** Tap water can contain organisms safe to swallow but dangerous in the nose.

### Hypertonic saline — Weak-or-mixed

**Finding:** May reduce swelling slightly more than isotonic saline but can sting and irritate.

**Mechanism:** Osmotic draw from swollen tissue.

**Practical use:** Use a labelled product rather than improvising very salty mixtures.

**Cautions:** Stop if significant burning or bleeding.

## Practical steps

- Wash hands before preparing or using the device.
- Boil tap water at a rolling boil for at least 1 minute, cool it, or use sterile/distilled water.
- Clean and air-dry the device after each use.
- Discard old homemade solution rather than storing it indefinitely.

## Who should avoid it or take extra care

- Do not irrigate a completely blocked nose with force.
- Seek advice after recent sinus/ear surgery or with immune compromise.
- Stop if irrigation repeatedly causes ear pressure, bleeding or significant pain.

## Common myths

- **Myth:** Australian tap water is automatically safe for neti pots.  
  **Reality:** Drinking-water standards do not make water sterile enough for nasal irrigation.
- **Myth:** Saline washes viruses out and prevents infection with certainty.  
  **Reality:** It mechanically clears material and may ease symptoms, but evidence for preventing or shortening infection is limited.

## Red flags

- Severe headache, fever, vomiting, confusion or stiff neck after unsafe nasal rinsing
- Persistent nosebleed or severe ear pain
- Eye swelling or vision changes

## Unsettled science / evidence gaps

- Best volume, tonicity and frequency for acute viral illness are not firmly established.
