# 5. Fatigue and body aches

**Section:** A. Symptoms  
**Reviewed:** 2026-07-15  

> **Educational information only.** This page does not diagnose illness or replace advice from a doctor, pharmacist, nurse or emergency service.

## Summary

Fatigue and muscle aches are common in influenza and other viral infections. The main aims are pain relief, hydration, sleep and a gradual return to activity rather than pushing through or remaining completely inactive for days.

## Evidence snapshot

Paracetamol and NSAIDs help pain and fever. Evidence that supplements or special foods rapidly restore energy is poor.

## What actually helps

### Sleep and reduced workload — Moderate

**Finding:** Supports recovery and reduces the chance of overexertion when acutely unwell.

**Mechanism:** Sleep supports immune regulation and pain tolerance.

**Practical use:** Cancel non-essential tasks, take brief naps if needed, and protect a full night of sleep.

**Cautions:** Prolonged bed rest can increase stiffness and deconditioning.

### Paracetamol or ibuprofen — Strong

**Finding:** Reduces headache, muscle aches and fever-related discomfort.

**Mechanism:** Analgesic and antipyretic effects.

**Practical use:** Use the lowest effective dose for the shortest time and follow the label.

**Cautions:** See medicine cautions in the OTC module.

### Gentle movement when able — Moderate

**Finding:** Short walks around the home and stretching can reduce stiffness after the worst phase has passed.

**Mechanism:** Maintains circulation and mobility.

**Practical use:** Stop if dizzy, breathless, faint or significantly worse afterward.

**Cautions:** Avoid vigorous exercise with fever, chest symptoms or marked systemic illness.

### Energy drinks and 'immune boosters' — Weak-or-mixed

**Finding:** Do not treat viral fatigue; high caffeine or sugar may worsen sleep, palpitations or dehydration.

**Mechanism:** —

**Practical use:** Prefer normal fluids and food.

**Cautions:** Check stimulant and supplement interactions.

## Practical steps

- Use a simple rhythm: drink, eat a little, rest, briefly move, then reassess.
- Return to exercise gradually after fever has resolved and energy is clearly improving.
- Expect influenza fatigue to last longer than a mild cold.

## Who should avoid it or take extra care

- New weakness affecting one side, severe muscle pain with dark urine, fainting or chest pain is not routine viral fatigue.
- Persistent exhaustion after the acute illness may need review for complications or another diagnosis.

## Common myths

- **Myth:** Exercise will sweat the virus out.  
  **Reality:** Hard exercise during fever or systemic illness adds physiological stress and may worsen symptoms.
- **Myth:** Sugar or vitamins provide instant immune energy.  
  **Reality:** Correcting a deficiency matters; megadoses during an ordinary cold rarely produce a rapid clinical effect.

## Red flags

- Chest pain, fainting or severe shortness of breath
- Severe weakness, confusion or inability to stay awake
- Dark urine with severe muscle pain
- Symptoms improving then sharply deteriorating

## Unsettled science / evidence gaps

- The best graded return-to-exercise protocol after uncomplicated influenza has limited direct trial evidence.
