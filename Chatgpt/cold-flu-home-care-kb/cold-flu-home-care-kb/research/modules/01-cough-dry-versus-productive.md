# 1. Cough: dry versus productive

**Section:** A. Symptoms  
**Reviewed:** 2026-07-15  

> **Educational information only.** This page does not diagnose illness or replace advice from a doctor, pharmacist, nurse or emergency service.

## Summary

A cough is a protective reflex, not a diagnosis. A dry, irritating cough may respond to soothing or short-term suppression, while a productive cough is usually better managed by hydration, movement and treating the cause rather than trying to stop all coughing.

## Evidence snapshot

Most acute viral coughs improve without specific treatment, although a post-viral cough can last 2–3 weeks. Evidence for OTC cough medicines is inconsistent.

## What actually helps

### Honey for people older than 12 months — Moderate

**Finding:** May modestly reduce night-time cough and improve sleep, especially in children, but it does not cure the infection.

**Mechanism:** Coats and soothes irritated throat tissue; may have mild antimicrobial activity.

**Practical use:** Use a small spoonful directly or in a warm drink before bed. Exact amounts have varied across trials.

**Cautions:** Never give honey to a child under 12 months. Consider sugar exposure and diabetes.

### Warm fluids and throat soothing — Moderate

**Finding:** Warm drinks, lozenges and frequent sips can reduce throat-triggered coughing and improve comfort.

**Mechanism:** Moistens irritated mucosa and may reduce cough-receptor stimulation.

**Practical use:** Sip regularly; avoid drinks hot enough to burn.

**Cautions:** Use age-appropriate lozenges; choking risk in young children.

### Dextromethorphan — Weak-or-mixed

**Finding:** Some adults report short-term reduction in dry cough, but systematic-review evidence is inconsistent and benefit is often small.

**Mechanism:** Acts centrally to reduce the cough reflex.

**Practical use:** Use only for a troublesome dry cough and follow the product label; check all combination ingredients.

**Cautions:** Avoid combining with interacting serotonergic medicines without pharmacist advice; may cause drowsiness, dizziness or misuse.

### Guaifenesin — Weak-or-mixed

**Finding:** May make mucus easier to cough up for some adults, but evidence for acute viral cough is limited.

**Mechanism:** Intended to increase hydration of airway secretions.

**Practical use:** Take with adequate fluid and follow the label.

**Cautions:** Not a substitute for assessment when breathless, wheezing or coughing blood.

### Routine cough mixtures in children — Weak-or-mixed

**Finding:** They do not reliably shorten illness and can cause harm; Australian guidance advises against decongestants, antihistamines and cough syrups for children with ordinary colds.

**Mechanism:** —

**Practical use:** Use saline, fluids, comfort and age/weight-appropriate pain relief instead.

**Cautions:** Do not improvise adult products or doses for children.

## Practical steps

- Decide whether the cough is mainly dry/tickly or wet/productive.
- For a wet cough, encourage fluids and gentle upright activity; do not aim to suppress every cough.
- For a dry night cough, use warm fluid, honey if over 12 months, and elevate the upper body slightly.
- Keep smoke, vaping, fragrances and cold dry air away from irritated airways.

## Who should avoid it or take extra care

- Seek pharmacist or doctor advice before cough suppressants if pregnant, breastfeeding, taking antidepressants, or living with asthma/COPD.
- Green or yellow phlegm alone does not prove a bacterial infection.
- A cough after a cold can linger, but worsening breathlessness or new fever needs reassessment.

## Common myths

- **Myth:** A productive cough should always be suppressed.  
  **Reality:** Coughing helps clear secretions. Suppression may be useful only when the cough is exhausting, painful or preventing sleep, and the person has no concerning symptoms.
- **Myth:** Cough syrup makes a cold end faster.  
  **Reality:** Most products only target symptoms, and the overall evidence for acute cough mixtures is weak or inconsistent.

## Red flags

- Trouble breathing, blue lips, stridor, severe wheeze or chest pain
- Coughing blood
- Confusion, collapse or marked drowsiness
- A child with chest recession, very fast breathing, poor feeding or fewer wet nappies
- Cough lasting more than a few weeks, or improving then clearly worsening

## Unsettled science / evidence gaps

- Which specific formulations and doses of dextromethorphan or guaifenesin provide a clinically meaningful benefit remains uncertain.
