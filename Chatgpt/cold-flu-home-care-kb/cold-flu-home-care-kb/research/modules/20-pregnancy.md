# 20. Pregnancy

**Section:** C. Special populations  
**Reviewed:** 2026-07-15  

> **Educational information only.** This page does not diagnose illness or replace advice from a doctor, pharmacist, nurse or emergency service.

## Summary

Pregnancy changes both influenza risk and medicine choices. Use the fewest medicines needed, check active ingredients, and seek early medical advice for suspected influenza or significant breathing symptoms.

## Evidence snapshot

Paracetamol is generally the preferred medicine for pain or fever in pregnancy when used as directed. Influenza antivirals are most useful when started early, and pregnancy is a recognised higher-risk state.

## What actually helps

### Rest, fluids, saline spray and warm drinks — Moderate

**Finding:** Low-risk measures can ease congestion, throat discomfort and dehydration.

**Mechanism:** Hydration, mechanical clearance and soothing.

**Practical use:** Use sterile commercial saline or correctly prepared rinse water; drink to thirst.

**Cautions:** Avoid burns from steam and avoid assuming herbal products are pregnancy-safe.

### Paracetamol — Strong

**Finding:** Relieves fever and pain and is generally considered suitable in pregnancy at recommended doses.

**Mechanism:** Analgesic and antipyretic.

**Practical use:** Use the lowest effective dose for the shortest necessary time and check combination products.

**Cautions:** Seek advice if fever persists or frequent dosing is needed; overdose can seriously injure the liver.

### Ibuprofen and other NSAIDs — Weak-or-mixed

**Finding:** They are not routine first-line cold/flu medicines in pregnancy and may be harmful at some stages, particularly later pregnancy.

**Mechanism:** Reduces prostaglandins.

**Practical use:** Use only if a clinician who knows the pregnancy recommends it.

**Cautions:** Risks vary by gestation and maternal conditions.

### Decongestants — Weak-or-mixed

**Finding:** Systemic decongestants have cardiovascular effects and limited pregnancy safety certainty; saline is preferred.

**Mechanism:** Vasoconstriction reduces nasal swelling.

**Practical use:** Ask a pharmacist, GP or obstetric clinician before oral or medicated nasal decongestants.

**Cautions:** Extra caution with hypertension, palpitations, placental problems or interacting medicines.

### Prescription influenza antivirals — Strong

**Finding:** Early treatment can reduce influenza severity and complications; pregnancy is a high-risk group in whom prompt assessment is important.

**Mechanism:** Inhibits influenza viral replication.

**Practical use:** Contact a clinician promptly when influenza is suspected rather than waiting several days.

**Cautions:** Prescription-only; dosing and choice require clinical assessment.

## Practical steps

- Check every ingredient in a cold-and-flu product rather than relying on the brand name.
- Tell the pharmacist the gestational week and all medicines and supplements being used.
- Test for COVID-19 or influenza when advised, especially with fever, marked aches or exposure.
- Keep taking prescribed asthma or other chronic-disease medicines unless the treating clinician advises a change.

## Who should avoid it or take extra care

- Avoid multi-ingredient products unless each ingredient is needed and confirmed appropriate.
- Do not use herbal remedies, high-dose vitamins or essential oils internally simply because they are 'natural'.
- Avoid overheating, dehydration and delaying assessment for breathlessness or reduced fetal movement.

## Common myths

- **Myth:** No medicine at all is safer than treating a fever.  
  **Reality:** Untreated significant fever can also matter; paracetamol is generally preferred when needed, alongside clinical advice.
- **Myth:** Natural cold remedies are automatically safe in pregnancy.  
  **Reality:** Many have limited pregnancy data, variable ingredients and possible interactions.

## Red flags

- Breathing difficulty, chest pain, fainting, blue lips or severe wheeze
- Persistent high fever, inability to keep fluids down or signs of dehydration
- Reduced fetal movement, contractions, bleeding or any obstetric concern
- Rapid deterioration or suspected influenza in someone with additional risk factors

## Unsettled science / evidence gaps

- Pregnancy safety data are sparse for many complementary products and some OTC decongestants; absence of evidence is not evidence of safety.
