# 9. Hydration

**Section:** B. Interventions  
**Reviewed:** 2026-07-15  

> **Educational information only.** This page does not diagnose illness or replace advice from a doctor, pharmacist, nurse or emergency service.

## Summary

Hydration prevents dehydration and can make thick secretions easier to manage, but drinking beyond thirst does not flush out viruses or dramatically shorten a cold. The goal is normal hydration, not forced litres of fluid.

## Evidence snapshot

Strong physiological rationale for replacing fluid losses; limited direct evidence that extra fluid changes cold duration.

## What actually helps

### Water and usual fluids — Strong

**Finding:** Maintains circulation, urine output and mucosal moisture.

**Mechanism:** Replaces fluid losses.

**Practical use:** Drink to thirst and take extra small amounts with fever, sweating, vomiting or poor intake.

**Cautions:** People with heart failure, kidney disease or fluid restrictions need individual advice.

### Warm drinks and soup — Moderate

**Finding:** Can soothe the throat, provide fluid and make nasal airflow feel easier.

**Mechanism:** Warmth and sensory effects may reduce discomfort.

**Practical use:** Choose low-salt options when sodium restriction matters.

**Cautions:** Very salty soups can be unsuitable for some heart or kidney conditions.

### Oral rehydration solution — Strong

**Finding:** Useful when vomiting, diarrhoea or poor intake causes meaningful fluid and electrolyte loss.

**Mechanism:** Provides glucose and electrolytes in a ratio that supports intestinal absorption.

**Practical use:** Use a commercial oral rehydration product mixed exactly as directed.

**Cautions:** Sports drinks and juice are not equivalent for significant dehydration.

### Excessive forced water — Strong

**Finding:** Does not cure infection and can rarely cause dangerous low sodium.

**Mechanism:** —

**Practical use:** Use thirst, urine colour and output as guides rather than a fixed high target.

**Cautions:** Higher risk in endurance athletes, kidney disease and people taking some medicines.

## Practical steps

- Aim for pale-yellow urine and regular urination.
- Offer small frequent drinks rather than a large glass when nauseated.
- For children, count wet nappies and watch tears, mouth moisture and alertness.
- Add fluid through ice blocks, soup or fruit when plain water is unappealing.

## Who should avoid it or take extra care

- Severe dehydration may require urgent medical treatment, not just more home fluids.
- Dry mouth alone can also result from mouth breathing or medicines.

## Common myths

- **Myth:** Eight or more glasses will flush the virus out.  
  **Reality:** Normal hydration supports body function; the immune system clears the virus.
- **Myth:** Milk creates mucus.  
  **Reality:** Milk can leave a temporary coating sensation but has not been shown to increase respiratory mucus production.

## Red flags

- Very little or no urine
- Sunken eyes, cold hands/feet, fast breathing or fast heart rate
- Confusion, marked drowsiness or inability to drink
- Infant with fewer wet nappies, no tears or sunken fontanelle

## Unsettled science / evidence gaps

- Trials have not established an ideal fluid volume specifically for uncomplicated colds.
