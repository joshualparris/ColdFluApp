# 22. Asthma, COPD and chronic respiratory disease

**Section:** C. Special populations  
**Reviewed:** 2026-07-15  

> **Educational information only.** This page does not diagnose illness or replace advice from a doctor, pharmacist, nurse or emergency service.

## Summary

Viral respiratory infections can trigger an asthma or COPD flare. Follow the existing action plan, continue prescribed preventer treatment and act early when breathing changes.

## Evidence snapshot

Reliever and preventer medicines prescribed for the underlying disease are more important than unproven cold remedies. Breathlessness, wheeze or reduced response to reliever medicine lowers the threshold for urgent assessment.

## What actually helps

### Follow the written asthma or COPD action plan — Strong

**Finding:** Structured escalation helps recognise and treat a flare early.

**Mechanism:** Bronchodilation and control of airway inflammation.

**Practical use:** Use reliever and other action-plan steps exactly as prescribed; check technique and spacer use.

**Cautions:** An action plan is individual; do not copy another person's doses.

### Continue prescribed preventer medicines — Strong

**Finding:** Stopping controller treatment can increase flare risk.

**Mechanism:** Maintains suppression of airway inflammation.

**Practical use:** Continue inhaled corticosteroids and other regular respiratory medicines unless a clinician advises otherwise.

**Cautions:** Rinse mouth after inhaled corticosteroids where directed.

### Influenza vaccination and early antiviral review — Strong

**Finding:** Chronic respiratory disease increases influenza complication risk.

**Mechanism:** Prevention and early antiviral action.

**Practical use:** Have annual vaccination and contact a clinician early for suspected influenza.

**Cautions:** Vaccination does not treat an existing illness.

### Saline, fluids and simple analgesia — Moderate

**Finding:** Can relieve upper-airway symptoms without stimulating the heart or drying secretions excessively.

**Mechanism:** Mechanical clearance, hydration and pain relief.

**Practical use:** Use saline products and paracetamol as appropriate; ask before NSAIDs if asthma-sensitive.

**Cautions:** Some people with asthma react to aspirin or NSAIDs.

### Steam, menthol and essential oils — Weak-or-mixed

**Finding:** May feel soothing but do not treat lower-airway narrowing and can irritate some people.

**Mechanism:** Sensory cooling/moisture rather than bronchodilation.

**Practical use:** Prefer a warm shower or clean humidifier rather than concentrated vapours.

**Cautions:** Stop if wheeze, cough or chest tightness worsens; avoid burn risks.

## Practical steps

- Check inhaler technique and spacer availability before illness worsens.
- Measure peak flow only if it is already part of the action plan.
- Avoid smoke, vaping, aerosols and strong fragrances.
- Seek same-day review if symptoms are moving into the chest or usual control is lost.

## Who should avoid it or take extra care

- Do not rely on cough suppressant or steam instead of asthma/COPD treatment.
- Use beta-blockers, NSAIDs and decongestants only with appropriate clinical context.
- Avoid sedating medicines if breathing is impaired or carbon-dioxide retention is a concern.

## Common myths

- **Myth:** Wheezing during a cold is just mucus and will clear with steam.  
  **Reality:** It may be airway narrowing that needs reliever treatment and medical review.
- **Myth:** Preventer inhalers should be stopped during an infection.  
  **Reality:** They are generally continued; stopping them can worsen control.

## Red flags

- Severe breathlessness, inability to speak full sentences, blue lips, exhaustion or confusion — call 000
- Reliever inhaler not lasting or not helping as the action plan predicts
- Marked chest tightness, rapidly falling peak flow or increasing work of breathing
- COPD flare with new confusion, drowsiness, chest pain or significant change in sputum

## Unsettled science / evidence gaps

- The value of home pulse-oximetry varies; readings can be inaccurate and should not override symptoms or an action plan.
