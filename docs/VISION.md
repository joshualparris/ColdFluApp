# Vision and principles

## Vision

Help anyone make safer, better-informed decisions about common respiratory illness by explaining what may help, what probably does not, what may cause harm, and when professional care is needed.

## The problem

Cold-and-flu advice is fragmented across health agencies, papers, product marketing, traditional practice, and social media. The same intervention may have different evidence for prevention, symptom relief, or shortening illness. Medication names, availability, contraindications, emergency pathways, and vaccination programs also vary by country.

The project will turn that fragmented material into small, traceable, independently reviewable modules. It is not intended to diagnose a user or replace a clinician.

## Core user promise

For every material claim, a reader should be able to understand:

- what outcome the intervention is claimed to affect;
- how confident the evidence is and why;
- whether the benefit is treatment, prevention, or comfort only;
- what practical use was actually studied;
- who should avoid it or seek individual advice;
- which jurisdiction the advice applies to;
- where the information came from and when it was reviewed.

## Product principles

1. **Safety outranks comprehensiveness.** “Every known remedy” is an intake goal, not a promise that every remedy deserves equal visibility or instructions.
2. **Evidence is claim-specific.** A remedy does not receive one permanent rating; each claimed outcome is rated separately.
3. **Uncertainty is content.** Conflicting, sparse, indirect, or low-quality evidence is shown plainly.
4. **Comfort is not cure.** Symptom comfort, physiological effects, disease duration, complication prevention, and transmission reduction remain distinct.
5. **Local rules are explicit.** Australian information is the first jurisdictional layer, never an unlabelled global default.
6. **Triage is prominent.** Emergency warnings and vulnerable-population advice must be reachable without reading a long article.
7. **Sources are inspectable.** References link to the exact guideline, review, paper, or regulatory record—not merely an organisation homepage.
8. **Freshness is visible.** Every published module has review dates, owners, and triggers for early reassessment.
9. **AI drafts; accountable humans approve.** Clinical and editorial approval cannot be automated away.
10. **Accessibility is part of correctness.** Plain language, semantic structure, keyboard support, readable contrast, and low-bandwidth operation are baseline requirements.

## Audience

Primary users are people managing mild respiratory symptoms at home and carers deciding what to do next. Secondary users include pharmacists, clinicians, educators, and researchers who want a concise evidence trail.

## Global usefulness

The core evidence layer can serve readers worldwide. Local overlays will provide country-specific medicine names and scheduling, emergency numbers, vaccination programs, care pathways, units, and regulator guidance. A country is not “supported” until its overlay has an accountable reviewer and freshness policy.

## Non-goals

- Diagnosing an individual illness or estimating personal risk.
- Replacing emergency services, a doctor, pharmacist, or poison information service.
- Recommending brands, selling supplements, or accepting paid rankings.
- Hosting user-generated treatment advice in the initial product.
- Collecting symptom or health data in the initial product.
- Publishing dosing calculators without separate clinical validation and risk review.

## Success

Early success means the content pipeline reliably prevents incomplete or unreviewed modules from publishing. Product success will later be measured through comprehension testing, accessibility conformance, source freshness, correction time, search usefulness, and whether users can accurately identify the correct level of care.
