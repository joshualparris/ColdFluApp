# Sore throat — risk-of-bias and applicability notes

No study-level risk-of-bias assessment has been completed. NICE's evidence appraisal is used as a lead, not a substitute for extraction of the underlying trials. Key risks are indirect adult-to-child transfer, product/formulation differences, small subjective pain-score effects, selective reporting, older trials, and conflating a guideline recommendation with effectiveness evidence. Healthdirect and the Australian Government pages are authoritative for local orientation but are not controlled effectiveness studies.

The Cochrane corticosteroid review is mostly adjunct-to-antibiotic evidence, with poor adverse-event reporting and only two paediatric trials. The ambroxol meta-analysis reports low study-reporting quality, short three-hour measurement and an active flavoured-lozenge comparison. Neither record supports a broad class recommendation for uncomplicated home self-care.

The Cochrane antibiotic review includes many older studies conducted under different diagnostic practices and complication risks; relative effects cannot be assumed to produce the same absolute benefit in contemporary Australian community care. The BMJ corticosteroid synthesis draws substantially from emergency/clinical settings and does not establish safety of repeated treatment. The AMC/DCBA and ambroxol reviews are formulation-specific; class extrapolation is inappropriate. Because full trial reports were unavailable for several proprietary products, study-level domain judgements remain for the independent evidence reviewer. The conservative response was to remove medicine/lozenge reader claims rather than assign unsupported certainty.
